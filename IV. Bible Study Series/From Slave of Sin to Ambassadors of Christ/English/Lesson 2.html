“Good [morning/afternoon/evening], everyone! Welcome to our weekly Bible Study. This is a time we set apart each week to strengthen the foundation of our faith in the Word of God.

The Bible reminds us in 2 Timothy 3:16–17 that ‘All Scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness; that the man of God may be perfect, thoroughly furnished unto all good works.’

Our goal in this Bible Study is not only to gain knowledge, but to allow God’s Word to transform our lives, shape our character, and draw us closer to Christ.

As we meet each week, we will grow together in understanding, encourage one another in faith, and prepare ourselves to stand firm in the truth of God’s Kingdom.

Let us approach this time with open hearts, ready to listen, to learn, and to apply His Word in our daily walk.”

Bible Study Facilitator’s Instructions

More...
  1. Opening Prayer
    • Begin the session by leading in prayer.
    • Ask for the Holy Spirit’s guidance, wisdom, and open hearts as you study God’s Word.
  2. Fellowship Check-in
    • Greet the group warmly.
    • Ask each participant how they are and briefly recall what they learned from the previous lesson.
    • Encourage them to share short reflections or personal applications.
  3. Reading of the Foundation Verses
    • Assign participants to read the Bible passages aloud, one by one.
    • Example: “Let’s have [Name] read Matthew 24:14, then [Name] read Mark 13:10…”
    • Make sure everyone follows along in their Bibles.
  4. Expansion and Explanation of the Verses
    • After the verses are read, explain the meaning of the passage.
    • Highlight the context, the message, and how it connects to God’s plan and the end times.
    • Allow space for participants to share their initial thoughts or insights.
  5. Explaining the Key Points
    • Present the prepared key points one by one.
    • Relate each point back to the verses that were read.
    • Emphasize the lessons that are practical and applicable to daily Christian living.
  6. Discussion/ Questions
    • Read each question slowly and clearly.
    • Allow participants to respond freely—listen actively and encourage open sharing.
    • Guide the discussion so it remains rooted in the Word of God.
    • Example: “According to Matthew 24:14, what must happen before the end will come?”
  7. Closing Prayer
    • Summarize the lesson briefly, reminding participants of the main message.
    • Invite prayer requests from the group.
    • Close with a prayer, asking God to help everyone apply the message in their lives and to be faithful in sharing the Gospel.

Series Title:
From Slave of Sin to Ambassadors of Christ


Lesson 2: The Importance and Meaning of Being a Servant of God


Introduction

After Christ set us free from sin, He did not leave us without direction. He called us to become “servants of God.” Far from being a negative concept, this is an honor and a mark of true freedom. To be a servant of God means a complete surrender of our lives, walking in holiness, and living for Him.

Teaching Points

  1. From Slavery to Sin to Servanthood of God

    • Once we were bound by sin, but now we are called to be servants of righteousness (Romans 6:17–18).

  2. The Meaning of Being a Servant of God

    • Complete surrender of self (Matthew 16:24).

    • Recognizing that He is the true owner of our lives (1 Corinthians 6:19–20).

    • Submitting to His righteousness and holiness.

  3. The Privileges of Being God’s Servant

    • Freedom from the chains of sin.

    • Growth in holiness and transformation of character.

    • The hope of eternal life (Romans 6:22).

  4. Examples of God’s Servants

    • Mary: “Behold, the servant of the Lord.” (Luke 1:38)

    • Paul: “Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus.” (Romans 1:1)

    • James: “James, a servant of God and of the Lord Jesus Christ.” (James 1:1)

    • Peter: “Simon Peter, a servant and apostle of Jesus Christ.” (2 Peter 1:1)

    • Jude: “Jude, a servant of Jesus Christ and a brother of James.” (Jude 1:1)

    • John: “The revelation of Jesus Christ, which God gave Him… and He made it known by sending His angel to His servant John.” (Revelation 1:1)

    • Moses: “So Moses the servant of the Lord died there in the land of Moab.” (Deuteronomy 34:5)

    • Joshua: “After the death of Joshua son of Nun, the servant of the Lord…” (Joshua 24:29)

    • David: Often referred to by God as “My servant David.” (2 Samuel 7:8; Psalm 89:20)


Scriptural Basis

  • Romans 6:22 – “But now that you have been set free from sin and have become slaves of God, the benefit you reap leads to holiness, and the result is eternal life.”

  • 1 Corinthians 6:19–20 – “You are not your own; you were bought at a price.”

  • Luke 1:38 – “Behold, I am the servant of the Lord; let it be to me according to your word.”

Discussion Questions

  1. What is the difference between slavery to sin and servanthood to God?

  2. Why does Paul call being a servant of God true freedom?

  3. In what ways did Mary and Paul show complete surrender to the Lord?

  4. What areas of your life are you still struggling to fully surrender to God?

Application / Challenge

  1. Personal Reflection: Who are you truly following each day—yourself, sin, or God?

  2. Faith Response: Bring to prayer the areas of your life not yet submitted to Him.

  3. Action Step: Take one concrete step of obedience this week—such as forgiving, changing an attitude, or serving.

Conclusion

Being a servant of God is not the loss of freedom but the only path to true freedom. It is a mark of humility and total surrender to Him. In our servanthood to God, we discover holiness, peace, and the assurance of eternal life. Let us boldly declare: “I am a servant of the Lord, and this is the highest honor.”
