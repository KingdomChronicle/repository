Romans 2:15

ESV (English Standard Version)

15They show that the work of the law is written on their hearts, while their conscience also bears witness, and their conflicting thoughts accuse or even excuse them


GUIDE REFLECTION:


The Law Written on the Heart: God's Moral Compass in a Confused World

From the moment we are born, we carry an innate sense of right and wrong. This verse highlights that morality is not something learned solely from society but is embedded within us by God. Even those who have never read the Bible still exhibit an understanding of justice, honesty, and compassion. This universal moral awareness suggests a divine Creator and leads to important questions: Why do all cultures uphold basic ethical principles? Why do people experience guilt when they do something wrong? And how should Believers respond to this truth in today’s morally confused world?

God has designed every human with a conscience—a built-in guide that either approves or condemns our actions. Even in secular societies, values such as fairness, human rights, and justice are universally recognized, reflecting God’s moral law written on human hearts.

Romans 2:14-15 ESV
14 For when Gentiles, who do not have the law, by nature do what the law requires, they are a law to themselves, even though they do not have the law. 15 They show that the work of the law is written on their hearts, while their conscience also bears witness, and their conflicting thoughts accuse or even excuse them

For example, a person who has never read the Bible still knows that stealing is wrong.

Romans 1:19-20 ESV
19 For what can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them. 20 For his invisible attributes, namely, his eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world, in the things that have been made. So they are without excuse.

The verse further affirms that God’s existence and moral nature are evident in creation, leaving humanity without excuse.

However, while conscience is a gift from God, it is not perfect.


  1. It can become seared through repeated sin

    1 Timothy 4:2 ESV
    through the insincerity of liars whose consciences are seared,

  2. Misguided by cultural norms

    Acts of the Apostles 26:9 NLT
    I used to believe that I ought to do everything I could to oppose the very name of Jesus the Nazarene.

  3. Inconsistent—where people justify their own sins while condemning others.

    Romans 2:1 ESV
    Therefore you have no excuse, O man, every one of you who judges. For in passing judgment on another you condemn yourself, because you, the judge, practice the very same things.

This explains why even morally upright individuals still need Christ; conscience alone is not enough to save us.

In today’s world, morality is often subjective, and absolute truth is rejected. Yet, Believers can use the universal moral instincts found in all people as a bridge to discuss faith. When someone argues for human rights or justice, they are unknowingly affirming God's law. Thoughtful discussions can lead to deeper questions: If we all recognize certain things as truly evil, what standard are we using to judge them?

At the same time, Believers must guard their own consciences. Society normalizes sin—whether greed, sexual immorality, or dishonesty—making it easy for our moral sensitivity to weaken. To counter this, believers must renew their minds with Scripture and seek godly accountability.

Romans 12:2 ESV
Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind, that by testing you may discern what is the will of God, what is good and acceptable and perfect.

Furthermore, we must show grace to those whose consciences are confused due to their upbringing or culture. Even the apostle Paul once persecuted Believers, believing he was doing right.

Acts of the Apostles 23:1 NLT
Gazing intently at the high council, Paul began: “Brothers, I have always lived before God with a clear conscience!”

Patiently guiding others to truth, is key in helping them realign their conscience with God’s Word.

2 Timothy 2:25 NLT
Gently instruct those who oppose the truth. Perhaps God will change those people’s hearts, and they will learn the truth.

Ultimately, while our conscience reveals God’s standards, it does not offer salvation. It merely points to our need for a Savior. Scripture makes it clear that all have sinned (Romans 3:23) and that even our best deeds are imperfect before God.

Isaiah 64:6 NKJV
But we are all like an unclean thing, And all our righteousnesses are like filthy rags; We all fade as a leaf, And our iniquities, like the wind, Have taken us away.

The good news is that Christ fulfilled the Law for us,

Matthew 5:17 ESV
Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them.

And offers forgiveness to those who repent.

1 John 1:9 ESV
If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness.

Understanding that God's law is written in our hearts is a gift—a divine guide that leads us to Him. However, in a world where sin corrupts morality, we must remain vigilant. It is essential to listen to our conscience but always compare it to God's Word to ensure it aligns with the truth. We must also help others recognize that their sense of right and wrong is evidence of the existence of a Creator. Acknowledging the moral law can serve as a path to introducing them to the God who established it. Above all, we must proclaim the Gospel—the only remedy for sin and guilt. So, the next time you feel that you have done something wrong, remember: that voice is not merely a natural feeling—it is a merciful warning from God. And if someone doubts God but believes in right and wrong, ask them: “Where does that standard come from?” The answer to this question may lead them to the Gospel.
