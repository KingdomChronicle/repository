Romans 4:9-12

NIV (New International Version)

9 Is this blessedness only for the circumcised, or also for the uncircumcised? We have been saying that Abraham’s faith was credited to him as righteousness. 10 Under what circumstances was it credited? Was it after he was circumcised, or before? It was not after, but before! 11 And he received circumcision as a sign, a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised. So then, he is the father of all who believe but have not been circumcised, in order that righteousness might be credited to them. 12 And he is then also the father of the circumcised who not only are circumcised but who also follow in the footsteps of the faith that our father Abraham had before he was circumcised.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

The true children of Abraham are not merely those who bear the sign of circumcision, but those who possess the same faith that he had.

In this passage, Paul concludes his explanation regarding the relationship between faith and circumcision in Abraham's life.

He has already shown that Abraham became the father of believing Gentiles because he was declared righteous before he was circumcised.

Now, Paul explains that Abraham is also the father of the Jews—but not simply because they are circumcised.

There is something more important that they must possess:

the faith that Abraham had.

In other words, having an outward sign is not enough if there is no genuine faith in the heart.

A person may belong to the nation of Israel.

He may observe religious traditions and rituals.

He may possess every outward mark of religious identity.

But if he does not have the same faith as Abraham, he is not truly following Abraham's example.

This is Paul's important point.

Being close to the things of God does not automatically mean that we are close to God.

The true measure is not our religious identity but our faith.

The same challenge applies to believers today.

  • We may have attended church for many years.
  • We may have grown up in a Christian home.
  • We may know many Bible verses and biblical teachings.

But the question is not merely what we know about God.

The question is whether we trust God the way Abraham trusted Him.

Abraham believed even before he saw the fulfillment of God's promises.

He trusted God even when circumstances seemed impossible.

He believed because he knew the God who makes promises.

That is the kind of faith God desires to see in each of us.

Not a faith based on tradition.

Not a faith based on outward appearance.

But a faith that trusts God and follows Him.

In the end, what separates the true people of God from others is not the rituals they perform but the faith they possess.

CONCLUSION

Abraham became the father of believers not only because he was the ancestor of Israel, but because he became a model of faith.

And according to Paul, those who truly follow Abraham are those who walk in the same faith that he had.

  • Outward identity is not enough.
  • Religious tradition is not enough.
  • Being part of a group or community is not enough.

What matters is a heart that trusts in God.

In the end, the question is not how close we are to religion.

The question is:

Are we walking in the same path of faith that Abraham walked?
