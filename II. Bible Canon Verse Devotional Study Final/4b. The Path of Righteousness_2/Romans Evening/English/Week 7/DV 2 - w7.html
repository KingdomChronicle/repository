Romans 4:9-12

NIV (New International Version)

9 Is this blessedness only for the circumcised, or also for the uncircumcised? We have been saying that Abraham’s faith was credited to him as righteousness. 10 Under what circumstances was it credited? Was it after he was circumcised, or before? It was not after, but before! 11 And he received circumcision as a sign, a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised. So then, he is the father of all who believe but have not been circumcised, in order that righteousness might be credited to them. 12 And he is then also the father of the circumcised who not only are circumcised but who also follow in the footsteps of the faith that our father Abraham had before he was circumcised.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

After asking whether the blessing of forgiveness is only for the circumcised or also for the uncircumcised, Paul returns to the most important evidence—the Scriptures themselves.

He says:

“Abraham’s faith was credited to him as righteousness.”

This was not Paul’s opinion.

Nor was it a new teaching.

It was clearly written in Scripture.

Through this, Paul reminds us that the foundation of salvation has never been rituals, traditions, or outward religious signs.

From the time of Abraham, God’s way has always been faith.

It is significant that Scripture does not say Abraham was declared righteous because of his circumcision.

Nor was it because of his good works or because he became the father of a great nation.

The clear reason was his faith in God.

He believed God's promise and trusted His word.

And that faith was credited to him as righteousness.

This is important to understand because people naturally look for other grounds on which to gain God's acceptance.

Sometimes we think God will accept us more because of our religious activities.

At other times, we place our confidence in our good reputation, our knowledge of the Bible, or the number of years we have spent in the faith.

But Scripture repeatedly shows that a person is made righteous before God through faith.

This does not diminish the importance of obedience to God.

Rather, it establishes the proper order.

Faith is the root.

Obedience is the fruit.

We do not obey in order to be saved.

We obey because we trust God.

This was true in Abraham’s life.

Before there was any outward sign of his covenant with God, there was already faith in his heart.

And that was what God first saw and acknowledged.

CONCLUSION:

God does not first look at what we have done before He accepts us.

He looks at whether we trust Him.

Abraham’s example reminds us that true righteousness begins with faith, not with outward works.

When our hope is placed in God rather than in ourselves, we experience the grace that declares us righteous.

In the end, the question is not how much we have done for God, but this:

Like Abraham, is our faith resting on the promises of God, or on our own abilities and goodness?
