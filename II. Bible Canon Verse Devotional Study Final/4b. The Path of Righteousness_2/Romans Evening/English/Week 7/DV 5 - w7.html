Romans 4:9-12

NIV (New International Version)

Faith First before Circumcision

9 Is this blessedness only for the circumcised, or also for the uncircumcised? We have been saying that Abraham’s faith was credited to him as righteousness. 10 Under what circumstances was it credited? Was it after he was circumcised, or before? It was not after, but before! 11 And he received circumcision as a sign, a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised. So then, he is the father of all who believe but have not been circumcised, in order that righteousness might be credited to them. 12 And he is then also the father of the circumcised who not only are circumcised but who also follow in the footsteps of the faith that our father Abraham had before he was circumcised.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

Faith unites the people of God—not race, tradition, or outward identity.

After showing that Abraham was declared righteous before he was circumcised, Paul now explains why this is so important in God's plan.

The order of events was not accidental.

God had a purpose in allowing faith to come before circumcision.

Because of this, Abraham became the father not only of the Jews but of all who believe in God.

This means that belonging to God's family is not based on bloodline or ethnicity.

It is not based on culture or religious tradition.

Nor is it based on any outward sign.

It is based on faith.

Before Christ came, many Jews believed that Gentiles could not have the same standing before God unless they first became like the Jews.

But Paul demonstrates that Abraham's own example contradicts that idea.

If Abraham was declared righteous while still uncircumcised, then it follows that God can also declare righteous anyone who believes, even if they are not part of the nation of Israel.

This is a wonderful truth for all of us.

  • Our background does not place us beyond the reach of God's grace.
  • Our ethnicity is not a barrier.
  • Our past is not a barrier.
  • Our social status is not a barrier.

What matters is a faith that comes to Christ.

In the gospel, we all come before God in the same way—as sinners in need of grace.

And we are all accepted through faith.

This is also a reminder that our unity as believers should not be based on outward things.

We may differ in culture, language, traditions, or background.

But:

  • We have one father in the faith.
  • We have one Savior.
  • We have received the same grace.
  • We share the same faith that brings us near to God.

CONCLUSION:

God made Abraham the father of all believers to demonstrate that salvation is available to everyone who comes to Him through faith.

It is not limited to one nation.

It is not reserved for one group of people.

It is open to all.

In the end, the question is not where we come from.

Nor is it what tradition we were raised in.

The important question is this:

Do we possess the same kind of faith that Abraham had—a faith that fully trusts in God and in His promises?
