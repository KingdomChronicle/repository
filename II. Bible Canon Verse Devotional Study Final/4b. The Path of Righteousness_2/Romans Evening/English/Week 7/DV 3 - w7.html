Romans 4:9-12

NIV (New International Version)

9 Is this blessedness only for the circumcised, or also for the uncircumcised? We have been saying that Abraham’s faith was credited to him as righteousness. 10 Under what circumstances was it credited? Was it after he was circumcised, or before? It was not after, but before! 11 And he received circumcision as a sign, a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised. So then, he is the father of all who believe but have not been circumcised, in order that righteousness might be credited to them. 12 And he is then also the father of the circumcised who not only are circumcised but who also follow in the footsteps of the faith that our father Abraham had before he was circumcised.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

God’s acceptance begins with faith, not outward works.

After proving that Abraham was declared righteous because of his faith, Paul now raises an important question:

When did this happen?

Was it before or after he was circumcised?

The answer from Scripture is clear:

Before he was circumcised.

This point is important because, for many Jews, circumcision was the sign of belonging to God's covenant people. It was one of the most significant aspects of their identity and obedience to the Law.

Yet Paul shows that long before God gave Abraham the sign of circumcision, God had already declared him righteous.

This means that Abraham’s relationship with God did not begin with a ritual.

It did not begin with a work.

It did not begin with obedience to an outward command.

It began with faith.

Abraham believed God's promise, and that was the reason he was counted as righteous.

This demonstrates that salvation is not based on any outward ceremony or religious activity.

We are not accepted by God because we are members of a church.

  • Not because we have been baptized.
  • Not because we do many good works.
  • Not because we have been religious for a long time.

All of these may be important as fruits of faith, but they are not the basis of our righteousness before God.

The basis is faith in Christ.

The same was true for Abraham.

  • Before the sign came, there was trust.
  • Before the work came, there was faith.
  • Before obedience came, there was belief in God.

In our day, there is a danger that religion can replace genuine faith.

We may be active in church activities yet not truly trust God.

We may practice religious rituals regularly while still not fully surrendering our lives to Christ.

This passage reminds us that true transformation begins in the heart.

Outward actions are important, but they are only the fruit of a believing heart.

We are not saved by our works.

Rather, because we have been saved through faith, we learn to obey.

CONCLUSION

Before Abraham received the sign of circumcision, he had already been accepted by God because of his faith.

This is a clear reminder that salvation does not begin with what we do, but with what we believe.

God does not accept us because we are good at obeying.

He accepts us because we trust Him.

And when faith is genuine, obedience will follow.

In the end, the question is not how much we have done for God.

The more important question is this:

Do we trust Christ as the only basis for our righteousness before God?
