Romans 4:1-8

NIV (New International Version)

Abraham Justified by Faith

1What then shall we say that Abraham, our forefather according to the flesh, discovered in this matter? 2 If, in fact, Abraham was justified by works, he had something to boast about—but not before God. 3 What does Scripture say? “Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness.”

4 Now to the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift but as an obligation. 5 However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness. 6 David says the same thing when he speaks of the blessedness of the one to whom God credits righteousness apart from works:

7 “Blessed are those whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered.8 Blessed is the one whose sin the Lord will never count against them.”

GUIDE REFLECTION:

After Paul explained that Abraham had nothing to boast about before God because he was not made righteous through his works, he now points to the clear testimony of Scripture:

“Abraham believed God, and because of this, God considered him righteous.”

This verse is extremely important because it reveals the foundation of a person’s relationship with God.

Scripture does not say that Abraham was considered righteous because of his goodness.

Neither does it say that he was declared righteous because of his obedience or accomplishments.

The clear reason was his faith.

Abraham believed what God said even before he saw its fulfillment.

God promised things that seemed impossible from a human perspective, yet Abraham chose to trust Him.

And it was that faith that God credited as righteousness.

This does not mean that Abraham was perfect.

Like every other person, he had weaknesses and failures.

Yet his acceptance before God was not based on his perfection but on his trust in a God who is faithful to His promises.

This is exactly what Paul wants his readers to understand.

If Abraham himself was declared righteous through faith, then there is no other way for us as well.

Salvation is not the result of our efforts to become good enough for God.

It is the result of trusting in what God has done for us.

Even today, many people try to become acceptable to God through good works, religious activities, or personal effort.

But Abraham’s example teaches us that true righteousness begins with faith.

It is not about what we can do for God, but about how much we trust His Word and His promises.

CONCLUSION:

Faith is not merely agreeing that God exists.

It is wholehearted trust in Him even when we have not yet seen everything He is doing.

Like Abraham, we are called to trust God and believe His promises.

And when we place our faith in Him, we demonstrate that our hope is not in ourselves but in His grace.

In the end, the question is not whether our works are sufficient for God to accept us, but this:

Like Abraham, are we willing to trust God even when what we see does not yet seem to match His promises?
