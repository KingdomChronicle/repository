Romans 4:1-8

NIV (New International Version)

Abraham Justified by Faith

1What then shall we say that Abraham, our forefather according to the flesh, discovered in this matter? 2 If, in fact, Abraham was justified by works, he had something to boast about—but not before God. 3 What does Scripture say? “Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness.”

4 Now to the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift but as an obligation. 5 However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness. 6 David says the same thing when he speaks of the blessedness of the one to whom God credits righteousness apart from works:

7 “Blessed are those whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered.8 Blessed is the one whose sin the Lord will never count against them.”

GUIDE REFLECTION:

In this passage, Paul further clarifies the difference between salvation based on works and salvation based on faith.

In the previous verses, he explained that wages are payment for work and not a gift. Now he shows how a person truly receives the righteousness of God.

He says:

“The one who does not rely on his own works but believes in God who justifies the ungodly...”

This statement is extremely important.

Paul does not say that God justifies those who are already perfect.

Neither does he say that God accepts only those who have done enough good works.

Instead, he presents the astonishing truth of the gospel:

God justifies sinners.

This is something many people struggle to accept.

Human nature tells us that we must first fix our lives before coming to God.

Some people think:

  • "Once I have changed myself, then I will come to God."
  • "Once I become a better person, then I will be worthy."
  • "Once I have made fewer mistakes, then God will accept me."

But the gospel says otherwise.

God does not wait for people to become righteous before He saves them.

He comes to those who acknowledge that they are sinners and need His grace.

Salvation is not a reward for the good.

It is a gift of grace for those who trust in God.

This does not mean that holiness or good works are unimportant.

Rather, they are the result of salvation, not the cause of salvation.

Like Abraham, a person is declared righteous not because of personal ability, but because of faith in God.

This is why no one can boast before Him.

Everything begins and ends with His grace.

Even today, many people continue to carry the heavy burden of trying to become good enough for God.

But the invitation of this passage is clear:

  • Stop trusting in yourself.
  • Come to God in faith.
  • Trust in His grace more than in your own ability.

CONCLUSION:

The good news of the gospel is that we do not have to pretend to be righteous in order to be accepted by God.

We simply need to recognize our need for Him and place our faith in the salvation He has provided.

God is not only the God of the righteous.

He is also the God who justifies sinners who come to Him in faith.

In the end, the question is not whether our goodness is sufficient for God to accept us, but this:

Is our trust placed in our own works, or in the God who justifies those who put their faith in Him?
