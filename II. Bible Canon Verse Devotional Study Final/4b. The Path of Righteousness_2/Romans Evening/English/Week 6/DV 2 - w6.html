Romans 4:1-8

NIV (New International Version)

Abraham Justified by Faith

1What then shall we say that Abraham, our forefather according to the flesh, discovered in this matter? 2 If, in fact, Abraham was justified by works, he had something to boast about—but not before God. 3 What does Scripture say? “Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness.”

4 Now to the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift but as an obligation. 5 However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness. 6 David says the same thing when he speaks of the blessedness of the one to whom God credits righteousness apart from works:

7 “Blessed are those whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered.8 Blessed is the one whose sin the Lord will never count against them.”

GUIDE REFLECTION:

In these verses, Paul continues to use Abraham as an example to demonstrate how a person is declared righteous before God.

He makes an important statement:

“If Abraham was declared righteous because of his works, he would have had something to boast about.”

At first glance, many might assume that Abraham was accepted by God because of his faithfulness, obedience, and good deeds. It is true that Abraham obeyed God on many occasions, but that was not the basis of his righteousness.

Paul shows that if righteousness could be obtained through human effort, then people would have reason to boast.

They could say:

  • “I deserve this because I did it.”
  • “God accepted me because I was good.”
  • “I earned this through my own efforts.”

But Paul clearly states:

“He had nothing to boast about before God.”

Why?

Because no matter how many good works a person may accomplish, they cannot erase the reality of sin.

Before a holy and perfect God, no one can stand and claim that their own righteousness is sufficient.

This is why salvation is a gift of grace and not a reward.

A person is not saved because they deserve it.

They are saved because God is merciful.

Even Abraham, the father of faith, had nothing to boast about before God.

His hope was not in what he had done but in the God whom he believed.

The same truth applies to us today.

Whenever we begin to think that we somehow deserve God's favor because of our service, goodness, or religious activities, we slowly shift our trust from Christ to ourselves.

But the gospel continually reminds us that everything we have is the result of God's grace.

CONCLUSION

Grace and pride cannot coexist.

When we truly understand that salvation is God's gift, every reason for boasting disappears and is replaced with gratitude.

Like Abraham, we have nothing to boast about before God except His goodness and faithfulness.

In the end, the question is not how much we have done for God, but this:

Is our confidence resting in our own works, or in the grace of God freely given through Jesus Christ?
