Romans 3:13-14

NIV (New International Version)

No One Is Righteous

9 What shall we conclude then? Do we have any advantage? Not at all! For we have already made the charge that Jews and Gentiles alike are all under the power of sin. 10 As it is written:

“There is no one righteous, not even one;11 there is no one who understands; there is no one who seeks God.12 All have turned away, they have together become worthless;there is no one who does good, not even one.”13 “Their throats are open graves; their tongues practice deceit.”“The poison of vipers is on their lips.”14 “Their mouths are full of cursing and bitterness.”15 “Their feet are swift to shed blood;16 ruin and misery mark their ways,17 and the way of peace they do not know.”18 “There is no fear of God before their eyes.”

19 Now we know that whatever the law says, it says to those who are under the law, so that every mouth may be silenced and the whole world held accountable to God. 20 Therefore no one will be declared righteous in God’s sight by the works of the law; rather, through the law we become conscious of our sin.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

As Paul continues his argument, he now describes sin not only as an inward condition, but as something that comes out and becomes visible through our words.

He draws from the Old Testament to show that this truth is not new—it has long been revealed in Scripture.

The imagery is strong and sobering:

“Their throats are open graves.”

The throat, which should be a source of life, truth, and praise to God, is instead described as a place of decay and death. This image shows that sin is never neutral—it is destructive.

Next, speech is described as deceitful, and the lips as having “the poison of vipers.” This reveals that human words are not only capable of lying, but also of harming, destroying, and corrupting others.

Then Paul intensifies the picture:

“Their mouths are full of cursing and bitterness.”

Here we see that sin is not only about actions or intentions, but also about how we use our words.

The mouth, which should be used for blessing, becomes a tool for anger, harm, and bitterness.

Paul is showing that sin is not only an “internal issue,” but something that spills out into our relationships with others—especially through our speech.

And within his larger argument, this becomes further evidence that no one can claim righteousness on their own. Because when we examine our words, we begin to see the reality of our condition before God.

CONCLUSION:

Our words are not small things—they reflect the condition of our hearts.

According to God’s Word, sin is seen not only in major actions, but even in the way we speak in daily life.

Paul’s message is not simply to condemn, but to show that no one has reason to boast in their own righteousness.

Because if even our words reveal imperfection, then it becomes even clearer how much we need God’s grace.

In the end, the question is not how good our words sound before people, but this:

Do our words reflect a heart being transformed by God, or a heart still in need of His transforming grace?
