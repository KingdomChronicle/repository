Romans 4:13-17

NIV (New International Version)

The Promise Comes Through Faith

13 It was not through the law that Abraham and his offspring received the promise that he would be heir of the world, but through the righteousness that comes by faith. 14 For if those who depend on the law are heirs, faith means nothing and the promise is worthless, 15 because the law brings wrath. And where there is no law there is no transgression.

16 Therefore, the promise comes by faith, so that it may be by grace and may be guaranteed to all Abraham’s offspring—not only to those who are of the law but also to those who have the faith of Abraham. He is the father of us all. 17 As it is written: “I have made you a father of many nations.” He is our father in the sight of God, in whom he believed—the God who gives life to the dead and calls into being things that were not.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

The Law Exposes Sin but Does Not Save

Paul continues his explanation about the purpose of the Law. After stating that the Law brings God's wrath upon those who violate it, he adds an important truth:

“And where there is no law there is no transgression.”

This does not mean that there is no sin where there is no Law. In the earlier chapters of Romans, Paul clearly taught that all people have sinned and are accountable to God. Even those who never received the Law of Moses are still responsible before God because He has revealed His truth through creation and through the human conscience.

So what does Paul mean?

His point is that there is a distinction between SIN and TRANSGRESSION. Sin is anything that falls short of God's holy standard, while transgression occurs when a clear command has been given and is deliberately disobeyed.

For example, a person may know that something is wrong, but when there is an explicit command that says, “Do not do this,” and the person does it anyway, the disobedience becomes more clearly exposed. Sin becomes an open violation of God's revealed will.

We can see this throughout biblical history. Even before the Law was given to Moses, people were already sinning.

  • Cain sinned when he murdered Abel.
  • The people in Noah's day sinned, and the flood came as judgment.
  • The people of Sodom and Gomorrah sinned and were judged by God.

This demonstrates that sin existed long before the Law was given.

However, when God gave the Law, it became much clearer what He commanded and what He forbade. As a result, human disobedience was more clearly identified as transgression against God.

In this way, the Law reveals how serious our disobedience is before God. The Law does not create sin; it simply exposes the sin that is already present. It is like a light shining into a dark room. The light does not create the dirt; it merely reveals what was already there but hidden from view.

This is also why the Law cannot be the way of salvation. The more clearly it reveals God's will, the more clearly it reveals our shortcomings. Instead of proving that we are righteous, it proves how desperately we need grace.

The Law is like a mirror. It does not wash a dirty face; it only reveals the dirt so that we recognize our need for cleansing. In the same way, the Law cannot remove our sin. It only shows us how much we need a Savior.

And here the beauty of the gospel shines once again. If salvation depended on our ability to obey every command of God, we would have no hope. But God made a way for us to be saved—not through our obedience to the Law, but through faith in Jesus Christ.

  • The Law reveals our need.
  • Christ provides the answer.

CONCLUSION

The Law is like a light that shines into the dark areas of our lives. It does not remove sin, but it reveals where the problem lies. And when we see our true condition, we begin to understand why we need the grace of God.

God did not save us because we had no transgressions.

He saved us because Christ paid for our transgressions.

In the end, when Paul says, “And where there is no law there is no transgression,” he does not mean that people are without sin when there is no Law. Rather, he means that there can be no direct violation of a clearly stated command where no such command has been given. But when God gave the Law, our disobedience became unmistakably clear, and our need for the grace found only in Jesus Christ was fully exposed.

Therefore, the question for each of us is not whether we have shortcomings before God—for it is clear that we do.

The question is:

Will we come to Christ, who offers forgiveness and new life to all who place their faith in Him?
