Romans 4:18-25

NIV (New International Version)

Abraham's Unwaivering Faith

18 Against all hope, Abraham in hope believed and so became the father of many nations, just as it had been said to him, “So shall your offspring be.” 19 Without weakening in his faith, he faced the fact that his body was as good as dead—since he was about a hundred years old—and that Sarah’s womb was also dead. 20 Yet he did not waver through unbelief regarding the promise of God, but was strengthened in his faith and gave glory to God, 21 being fully persuaded that God had power to do what he had promised. 22 This is why “it was credited to him as righteousness.” 23 The words “it was credited to him” were written not for him alone, 24 but also for us, to whom God will credit righteousness—for us who believe in him who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead. 25 He was delivered over to death for our sins and was raised to life for our justification.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

In these verses, Paul reveals the very heart of Abraham’s faith—his complete trust in the faithfulness of God.

“Being fully persuaded that God had power to do what he had promised.”

The word “fully” is important. This was not a shallow hope or mere positive thinking. It was a firm conviction that God is faithful and powerful enough to fulfill His Word.

As the years passed, Abraham saw no visible evidence that the promise was close to being fulfilled. Instead, as he and Sarah grew older, the situation appeared even more impossible from a human perspective.

Yet he did not measure God’s power by his circumstances.

Rather, his faith was rooted in who God is.

Abraham knew that God does not lie. God does not make promises that He cannot fulfill. Therefore, even when he saw no change, he continued to place his hope in the faithfulness of the God who had promised.

“This is why ‘it was credited to him as righteousness.’”

Not because he was perfect. We know that Abraham had weaknesses and made mistakes. Yet despite these things, he chose to trust God. And that trust was credited to him as righteousness.

Here we see the essence of the gospel: God does not grant righteousness to those who are perfect, but to those who trust in Him.

This is the same truth that we receive today.

We are not considered righteous because of our good works, religious efforts, or ability to obey the Law perfectly. We are declared righteous through faith in Jesus Christ.

Our salvation rests not on our ability, but on the finished work of Christ on the cross.

CONCLUSION:

God chose righteousness to be received through faith so that it would be clearly seen that salvation does not depend on human ability, but on His faithfulness and power to fulfill His promises.

Abraham was considered righteous not because he was perfect, but because he fully trusted in the God who never lies and never fails in His Word. Even though many years passed and the situation became increasingly impossible from a human perspective, he did not cling to what he could see but to the God who had promised.

No one can claim to be considered righteous because of personal goodness.

No one can boast that they received God’s promise because of their own effort or faithfulness.

Everything depends on the God who is faithful to His Word and on the grace He freely gives to those who believe.

And the same faith that connected Abraham to the righteousness of God is the faith to which we are called today—the faith in God who raised Jesus Christ our Lord from the dead, the One who was delivered over to death for our sins and raised to life for our justification.

Therefore, righteousness, forgiveness, and new life are not rewards for the deserving, but gifts given to those who trust in the God who fulfills His promises.

In the end, the question is not how good we are or how faithful we have been to God.

The question is:

Do we continue to place our full trust in the God who never fails to fulfill His promises through Jesus Christ our Lord?
