Romans 4:18-25

NIV (New International Version)

Abraham's Unwaivering Faith

18 Against all hope, Abraham in hope believed and so became the father of many nations, just as it had been said to him, “So shall your offspring be.” 19 Without weakening in his faith, he faced the fact that his body was as good as dead—since he was about a hundred years old—and that Sarah’s womb was also dead. 20 Yet he did not waver through unbelief regarding the promise of God, but was strengthened in his faith and gave glory to God, 21 being fully persuaded that God had power to do what he had promised. 22 This is why “it was credited to him as righteousness.” 23 The words “it was credited to him” were written not for him alone, 24 but also for us, to whom God will credit righteousness—for us who believe in him who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead. 25 He was delivered over to death for our sins and was raised to life for our justification.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

In this passage, Paul further describes the steadfastness of Abraham’s faith. He did not simply believe at the beginning—he continued to believe even as the years passed and the fulfillment of the promise seemed increasingly impossible.

“Without weakening in his faith, he faced the fact that his body was as good as dead…”

Abraham was not blind to his circumstances. He knew he was old. He also knew that Sarah was barren and had long passed the natural age for bearing children. From a human perspective, every possibility had already been exhausted.

Yet despite this, Abraham did not allow what he saw to become the foundation of his faith.

He did not deny reality, but neither did he allow reality to prevail over the promise of God.

Here we see the difference between faith and mere optimism. Optimism depends on possibility. Faith rests on the God who is able to do the impossible.

Many people gradually grow weak in faith when their circumstances do not change. When waiting becomes long, when prayers seem unanswered, or when situations appear to grow worse, the heart is easily tempted to doubt.

But Abraham demonstrates that true faith is not based on the intensity of the situation, but on the faithfulness of the God who made the promise.

He was not considered righteous because of his own ability, but because of his continuing trust in God even when there was no visible evidence that the promise would be fulfilled.

This is also the challenge for us today.

It is easy to trust when the answer is clear. But faith is truly revealed when the answer is not yet visible.

The God in whom we believe does not change. His Word does not weaken even when our circumstances seem to worsen.

CONCLUSION:

God chose righteousness to be received through faith so that it would be clearly seen that salvation and the fulfillment of His promises do not depend on human ability, but on His faithfulness and power.

Abraham was not considered righteous because of his strength, but because of his continuing trust in God even when everything he saw declared that the promise was impossible. And this faith was not merely temporary hope, but a firm confidence in the God who never changes.

No one can claim to have become righteous through personal effort.

No one can boast that he received God’s promise because of his own ability.

Everything depends on God who fulfills His Word.

And the same faith that connected Abraham to the righteousness of God is the faith appointed for us today—the faith in God who raised Jesus Christ our Lord from the dead, the One who was delivered over to death for our sins and raised to life for our justification.

Therefore, righteousness, forgiveness, and new life are not rewards for the deserving, but gifts given to those who believe.

In the end, the question is not how great our dreams or prayers may be.

The question is:

Do we continue to trust in the God who fulfills His promises, the God who raised Jesus Christ from the dead so that we might be declared righteous in His sight?
