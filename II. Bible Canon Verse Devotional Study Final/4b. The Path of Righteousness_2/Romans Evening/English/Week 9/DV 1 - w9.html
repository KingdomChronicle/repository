Romans 4:18-25

NIV (New International Version)

Abraham's Unwavering Faith

18 Against all hope, Abraham in hope believed and so became the father of many nations, just as it had been said to him, “So shall your offspring be.” 19 Without weakening in his faith, he faced the fact that his body was as good as dead—since he was about a hundred years old—and that Sarah’s womb was also dead. 20 Yet he did not waver through unbelief regarding the promise of God, but was strengthened in his faith and gave glory to God, 21 being fully persuaded that God had power to do what he had promised. 22 This is why “it was credited to him as righteousness.” 23 The words “it was credited to him” were written not for him alone, 24 but also for us, to whom God will credit righteousness—for us who believe in him who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead. 25 He was delivered over to death for our sins and was raised to life for our justification.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

In this passage, Paul presents one of the strongest examples of true faith—the faith of Abraham.

“Against all hope, Abraham in hope believed…”

From a human perspective, there was no longer any reason to hope. Abraham and Sarah were both old, and the ability to bear a child seemed impossible. Everything in his situation was shouting hopelessness.

Yet in spite of this, Abraham chose to believe—not based on what he saw, but on the Word of God.

His faith was not anchored on circumstances, age, or human logic, but on the character of God—the God who does not lie and always fulfills His promises.

When God said, “So shall your offspring be,” he had no child yet. But he considered God’s promise more real than his present reality.

Here we see true faith:

trusting God even when the fulfillment of His Word is not yet visible.

This does not mean Abraham was unaware of his situation. He fully understood the limitations of his body and the impossibility of the promise from a human standpoint. But he chose to elevate God’s truth above the limits of his experience.

This is also the challenge for us today.

There are many moments when our circumstances seem to contradict God’s promises. There are prayers we have waited on for a long time, doors that seem closed, and problems that appear to have no solution.

In these moments, God calls us not to base our faith on what we see, but on His Word.

Faith is not the denial of reality, but trust in the God who is greater than reality.

It is not believing that everything will be easy.

It is trusting that God is faithful even when we cannot yet see what He is doing.

CONCLUSION:

Abraham teaches us that true faith rests not on possibility, but on the power and faithfulness of the God who promises.

God is not limited by human limitations. What is impossible for us is never impossible for Him. That is why, like Abraham, we are called to trust—not because all answers are already clear, but because the God who gives the promise is faithful.

Yet in the deeper light of the gospel, Paul shows us that this faith is not only about trusting a promise, but about trusting the God who fulfilled His promise through Jesus Christ—who was given as the payment for our sins and raised again for our justification.

Here it becomes clear that salvation does not depend on man’s ability to reach God, but on God’s work of reaching man through Christ.

No one can claim to be deserving of this grace. No one can boast that it was achieved by personal effort. All of it is received as God’s gift through faith.

In the end, faith is not trust in our ability to understand everything God is doing, but trust in the God who is fully knowing, fully powerful, and fully faithful to His Word—proven through the cross and the resurrection of Jesus Christ.

And the question now is not how strong our ability to believe is, but where we place our full trust:

Do we trust in the God who fulfilled His promise through Jesus Christ, and are we willing to live the kind of faith Abraham had—focused not on what is seen, but on the God who never fails?
