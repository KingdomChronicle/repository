Romans 3:27-31

NIV (New International Version)

RIGHTEOUSNESS THROUGH FAITH

27 Where, then, is boasting? It is excluded. Because of what law? The law that requires works? No, because of the law that requires faith. 28 For we maintain that a person is justified by faith apart from the works of the law. 29 Or is God the God of Jews only? Is he not the God of Gentiles too? Yes, of Gentiles too, 30 since there is only one God, who will justify the circumcised by faith and the uncircumcised through that same faith. 31 Do we, then, nullify the law by this faith? Not at all! Rather, we uphold the law.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

In this passage, Paul clearly summarizes the central message of his explanation:

a person is declared righteous through faith and not through obedience to the Law.

This is the truth he repeatedly emphasized in the earlier verses. For if salvation were based on keeping the Law, no one would have any hope.

Why?

Because no one has perfectly obeyed it.

All have sinned. All have fallen short of God’s standard. Because of this, no one can claim that personal goodness is enough to make them acceptable before God.

But here we see the beauty of God’s grace.

Instead of leaving humanity in hopelessness, God provided a way for us to be declared righteous.

  • Not through more works.
  • Not through stricter obedience.
  • Not through greater effort.
  • But through faith in Jesus Christ.

Faith does not mean relying on our own ability to reach God. Rather, it means trusting that what Christ accomplished on the cross is sufficient to save us.

Sometimes people think they must “add” to what Jesus has done through their own goodness in order to be accepted by God.

But Paul shows that salvation is not a combined work between God and man.

God made the way, and humanity is invited to respond through faith.

CONCLUSION:

Salvation is not a reward for the person with the greatest number of good works, but a gift of grace received through faith.

God does not accept us because we have become sufficient.

He accepts us because Christ is sufficient.

Therefore, the Christian life is not an exhausting effort to earn God’s love, but a response of gratitude for the love He has already given.

In the end, the question is not how much we do for God, but this:

is our trust placed in our own works, or in the finished work of Jesus Christ on the cross?
