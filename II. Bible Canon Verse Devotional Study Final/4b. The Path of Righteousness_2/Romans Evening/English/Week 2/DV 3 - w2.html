Romans 3:1-4

NIV (New International Version)

1What advantage, then, is there in being a Jew, or what value is there in circumcision? 2 Much in every way! First of all, the Jews have been entrusted with the very words of God.

3 What if some were unfaithful? Will their unfaithfulness nullify God’s faithfulness? 4 Not at all! Let God be true, and every human being a liar. As it is written:

“So that you may be proved right when you speak and prevail when you judge.”

GUIDE REFLECTION:

Human failure never cancels God’s faithfulness.

In this part of the letter, Paul seems to be engaging in a kind of dialogue with an unseen questioner—someone raising objections to what he has been saying. After showing that all people are sinful, including the Jews who have the Law, a natural question follows: does it still have value to belong to God’s people?

Paul answers that yes—there is great advantage, especially because they were entrusted with the very words of God. But the discussion does not end there.

A deeper question arises: what if those entrusted with God’s Word were unfaithful? Does that mean God Himself is no longer faithful?

This is the critical issue Paul addresses. And his answer is not a mild denial—it is a strong correction: “Absolutely not!”

This question comes from a misunderstanding of the relationship between God and humanity. People often think God’s faithfulness is like a contract that can be broken when one side fails.

But Scripture shows that God’s faithfulness does not depend on human response. He is faithful not because we are faithful, but because that is who He is.

When people lie, God remains true. When people fail, God remains righteous. No human failure has the power to change who God is.

In fact, human failure only highlights God’s faithfulness—not as an excuse for sin, but as evidence that God is not like humans who change or give up.

This truth is fully revealed in Jesus Christ. In the face of repeated human failure, God did not withdraw from His promise.

Instead, He fulfilled it through Christ.

At the cross, two things that seem opposite are revealed together:

  • God’s justice against sin
  • and God’s faithfulness to His promise to save

Human sin did not cancel God’s plan. Instead, it became the very place where God displayed His faithfulness.

In personal life, it is easy to think that when we fail or sin, God’s attitude toward us changes or His promises become uncertain.

But this passage reminds us that God is not like humans. His faithfulness does not shift based on our condition.

This does not mean sin has no consequences. But it does mean that God remains righteous and true—and His promises do not disappear because of our failures.

CONCLUSION

In the midst of human unfaithfulness, God remains faithful.

He does not change. He does not withdraw His Word. And He does not allow human failure to redefine His truth.

In the end, the question is not whether God will be faithful—because He always is.

The question is how we will respond to that faithfulness in the face of our own weakness.
