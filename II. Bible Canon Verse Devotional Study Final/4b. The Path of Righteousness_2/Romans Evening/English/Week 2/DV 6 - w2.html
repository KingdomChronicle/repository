Romans 3:7-8

NIV (New International Version)

7 Someone might argue, “If my falsehood enhances God’s truthfulness and so increases his glory, why am I still condemned as a sinner?” 8 Why not say—as some slanderously claim that we say—“Let us do evil that good may result”? Their condemnation is just!

GUIDE REFLECTION:

God’s faithfulness is never a reason to make sin acceptable.

At this point, Paul pushes the argument to its most extreme form. If human lies somehow make God’s truthfulness more evident, why should the liar still be judged as a sinner? If sin ends up highlighting God’s glory, wouldn’t it make more sense to continue sinning?

This is the kind of thinking Paul is exposing—distorted logic that uses truth to justify sin.

This is not just a simple misunderstanding. Paul says that some people were even accusing him of teaching exactly this: that because human sin highlights God’s faithfulness, people are free to continue sinning. But his response is clear—it is slander.

The argument “do evil so that good may come” is not only wrong—it directly contradicts God’s nature.

God does not become holy because of human sin. He is holy in Himself. His truth does not depend on human falsehood to be true.

When a person sins, their intention is not to glorify God—they are following their own desires. Any “good outcome” that may come from it does not justify the wrong origin.

In other words, a wrong action does not become right just because the result seems good.

In Jesus Christ, God clearly shows how He deals with sin.

He does not encourage it. He does not make it acceptable. Instead, He judges it—but in His mercy, that judgment is taken by Christ on the cross.

Here we see that:

sin is serious before God justice is not set aside and grace is not a license to continue wrongdoing

The cross proves that God is both holy and merciful—He never uses evil as a means to achieve righteousness.

In our lives, we may not say this directly, but the same thinking can quietly shape our decisions.

There are times when we think:

  • “It’s okay, the result is good anyway.”
  • “It’s not that bad if it helps someone.”
  • “God will use it for good anyway.”

But this passage reminds us clearly: sin does not become right because of its outcome.

True obedience is not only about results, but about faithfulness in the way we act.

CONCLUSION

God’s faithfulness is not a license for sin, but an invitation to holiness.

Any attempt to make wrongdoing acceptable does not come from God, but from distorted human thinking.

In the end, we are not called to manipulate truth, but to obey it.
