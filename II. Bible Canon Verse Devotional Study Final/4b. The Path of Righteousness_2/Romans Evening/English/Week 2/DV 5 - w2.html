Romans 3:5-6

NIV (New International Version)

5 But if our unrighteousness brings out God’s righteousness more clearly, what shall we say? That God is unjust in bringing his wrath on us? (I am using a human argument.) 6 Certainly not! If that were so, how could God judge the world?

GUIDE REFLECTION:

God’s justice is never compromised, even if human sin highlights His holiness.

As Paul continues his argument, he introduces a sharper question—one that reflects a human way of reasoning that could be used to question God.

If human wickedness makes God’s righteousness more visible, does that mean sin is somehow “useful”? And if so, wouldn’t it be unjust for God to punish something that ends up revealing His holiness?

It is important to see that this is not Paul’s teaching, but a human argument he is exposing—an attempt to twist truth in order to justify sin.

And his response is clear and strong: “Absolutely not!”

This question reveals how easily human reasoning can distort truth in order to make sin seem acceptable.

At first glance, the argument may sound logical:

If God’s righteousness becomes clearer through human sin, why punish the sinner?

But the problem is not the logic—it is the assumption behind it.

The reasoning may appear convincing, but its foundation is wrong.

Sin never becomes good, even if it somehow results in the display of God’s righteousness. God is holy in Himself, not because of human sin.

More importantly, Paul highlights a key truth: if God were not just, He would not have the right to judge the world. The very idea of judgment is based on the reality that God is perfectly righteous.

He does not judge simply because He chooses to—He judges because He alone is the true standard of righteousness.

Only He can give perfectly just judgment, because nothing is hidden from Him—everything is open and exposed before His eyes.

In Jesus Christ, we see the perfect balance of God’s justice and grace.

At the cross, God did not ignore sin or treat it lightly just because something good could come from it. Instead, He revealed the seriousness of sin through His perfect justice—it had to be judged. Yet in an incredible act of grace, the judgment that belonged to humanity was placed upon Jesus Christ on the cross.

At the cross, perfect justice and perfect love meet: sin is truly judged, but sinners are given a way of salvation through His sacrifice.

Here we see:

God remains just and yet provides a way of salvation

He did not compromise His justice in order to show mercy. Instead, both His justice and mercy are fully revealed in one act.

In our daily lives, we may not say this argument directly, but we can sometimes think in similar ways.

There are moments when we:

justify wrongdoing because it leads to a “good outcome” minimize sin because it does not seem “that serious” or reduce the weight of wrongdoing because we have learned something from it

But this passage reminds us: sin remains sin, and God remains just.

CONCLUSION

God never becomes unjust, even when people try to question Him.

His judgment is right. His standard does not change. And His holiness is not dependent on human sin.

In the end, the issue is not whether God is just enough, because His justice is never in question.

The real question is whether we are willing to submit and accept that His standard is the true measure of right and wrong.
