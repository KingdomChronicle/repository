Romans 3:5-6

NIV (New International Version)

5 But if our unrighteousness brings out God’s righteousness more clearly, what shall we say? That God is unjust in bringing his wrath on us? (I am using a human argument.) 6 Certainly not! If that were so, how could God judge the world?

GUIDE REFLECTION:

God’s justice is never compromised, even if human sin serves to reveal His righteousness.

As Paul continues his argument, he brings up a sharper question—one that reflects a kind of reasoning people might use to challenge God.

If human sin somehow makes God’s righteousness more evident, does that mean sin is actually “helpful”? And if so, would it not seem unjust for God to punish the very thing that highlights His holiness?

It is important to recognize that this is not Paul’s teaching, but an argument he presents from a human perspective—a line of thinking that attempts to twist the truth.

And his answer is clear and emphatic: “Certainly not!”

This question reveals how easily people can distort truth in order to justify sin.

At first glance, the argument may sound logical:

If God’s holiness becomes clearer because of human sin, why punish the sinner?

But the issue is not the logic—it is the premise.

The reasoning may appear sound, but it is built on a false foundation.

Sin never becomes good, even if it results in revealing God’s holiness. God is holy in Himself, not because of human sin.

Furthermore, Paul highlights a crucial truth: if God were not just, He would have no right to judge the world. The very concept of judgment depends on the reality that God is perfectly righteous.

He does not judge arbitrarily—He judges because He alone is the true standard of righteousness.

He alone can render perfect judgment, because nothing is hidden from Him—everything is exposed before His eyes.

In Jesus Christ, we see the perfect balance of God’s justice and grace.

At the cross, God did not ignore sin or treat it lightly, as if it were “acceptable” just because something good might come from it. Instead, He demonstrated the weight and seriousness of sin through His holy justice—it had to be judged. Yet in a remarkable act of grace, the judgment that should have fallen on humanity was not removed, but fully placed upon Jesus Christ on the cross.

At the cross, perfect justice and perfect love met: sin was truly judged, yet the sinner was given a way to be saved through His sacrifice.

Here we see:

  • God remains just
  • yet He also provides a way for salvation

He did not compromise His justice in order to show mercy. Rather, both His justice and His mercy were revealed in one act.

In our daily lives, we may not openly express this kind of argument, but at times, our thinking follows the same pattern.

There are moments when:

  • we justify wrongdoing because it leads to a “good outcome”
  • we think our sin is “not that serious”
  • or we minimize wrongdoing because we “learned something” from it

But this passage reminds us: sin remains sin, and God remains just.

It also helps to view this through the lens of human relationships.

In a relationship, when someone commits a wrong, forgiveness may be given. But as the offense is repeated and becomes more serious, there may come a point where the other person finds it difficult—or even impossible—to forgive, especially when deeply hurt.

Even if the offender is truly repentant, it is still possible for a person to refuse forgiveness, because our patience and capacity to forgive have limits.

But God is not like that.

No matter how great or serious our sin may be, if we truly repent and turn away from it, His arms remain open to receive us.

He does not look merely at what we have done—He looks at a heart that is genuinely repentant.

This does not nullify His justice; rather, it shows how His justice works together with His grace.

CONCLUSION

God is never unjust, even if people try to find ways to question Him.

His judgment is right. His standard does not change. And His holiness does not depend on human sin.

In the end, the issue is not whether God is still just—His justice is not something that needs to be questioned or judged by man.

The more important question is this: are we willing to submit and accept that His standard is the true measure of what is right and wrong?
