Romans 3:1-4

NIV (New International Version)

1What advantage, then, is there in being a Jew, or what value is there in circumcision? 2 Much in every way! First of all, the Jews have been entrusted with the very words of God.

3 What if some were unfaithful? Will their unfaithfulness nullify God’s faithfulness? 4 Not at all! Let God be true, and every human being a liar. As it is written:

“So that you may be proved right when you speak and prevail when you judge.”

GUIDE REFLECTION:

God’s faithfulness is complete and is never affected by human unfaithfulness.

At this point in the letter, Paul deepens an important question: if those entrusted with God were unfaithful, does that affect God’s own faithfulness?

This question is easy to relate to human experience. In our world, faithfulness is often conditional. When one person fails, the other often pulls away as well. Relationships break, promises are lost, and trust collapses.

But it is not so with God.

In a strong declaration, Paul responds: “Absolutely not!” The original sense of this phrase is a firm rejection—like saying, “Impossible! That cannot happen!”

This shows that the very idea of God becoming unfaithful contradicts His nature.

God is faithful not because of human conditions, but because of who He is by nature.

When Paul says that “God is true, even if every human being is a liar,” he is making a sharp contrast: all of humanity may fail, but God never fails.

He does not change according to circumstances. He does not adjust to human weakness. And most importantly, He never breaks His Word.

The quotation from Psalm 51:4, which Paul references, reinforces this truth: every word of God is righteous, and His judgments are always right. When He speaks, it is true. When He judges, it is just.

In other words, God never makes mistakes—in what He says or what He does.

This faithfulness is fully revealed in Jesus Christ.

In spite of human sin and unfaithfulness, God did not withdraw His promise to save. Instead, He fulfilled it in the most profound way—through Christ.

At the cross, we see that:

  • God remained faithful to His Word about sin
  • and remained faithful to His promise of salvation

He did not ignore sin, but He also did not abandon humanity.

This is God’s faithfulness—not compromise, but fulfillment.

In our lives, there are times when human unfaithfulness can lead us to doubt. When we are hurt, exhausted, or disappointed, we may wonder: “Is God like this too?”

  • But this passage makes it clear: God is not like humans.
  • His faithfulness does not change even when we fail we doubt or we fall

This is not an excuse to take sin lightly, but an invitation to trust more deeply.

CONCLUSION

In a world full of changing and unreliable promises, God remains firm and true.

His faithfulness does not change.

It can be compared to a great umbrella—while we remain under it, we experience the protection, assurance, and unchanging faithfulness of God.

But because of our free will, we can choose to step out from under this umbrella.

When we do, it does not mean that God has changed or that He has become unfaithful. He remains faithful.

However, God is holy, and He cannot accept sin as part of His nature. So when we choose to remain in sin, it is we who distance ourselves from Him.

In simple terms, it is not God who leaves—it is we who willingly step out from under His umbrella.

And in doing so, we can no longer fully experience His faithfulness—not because it has diminished, but because we have chosen to separate ourselves.

So if we desire to experience the unchanging faithfulness of God, the invitation is clear: turn away from sin and return under His covering.

Let us not be deceived by a wrong understanding of God’s faithfulness. It is not permission to continue in sin, but an invitation to live in truth.

In the end, God is always faithful.

The question is: will we remain under His umbrella, or will we choose to walk away?
