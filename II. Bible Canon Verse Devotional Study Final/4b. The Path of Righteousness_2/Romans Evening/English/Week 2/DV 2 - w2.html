Romans 3:1-4

NIV (New International Version)

1What advantage, then, is there in being a Jew, or what value is there in circumcision? 2 Much in every way! First of all, the Jews have been entrusted with the very words of God.

3 What if some were unfaithful? Will their unfaithfulness nullify God’s faithfulness? 4 Not at all! Let God be true, and every human being a liar. As it is written:

“So that you may be proved right when you speak and prevail when you judge.”

GUIDE REFLECTION:

God’s gifts are real and valuable, but God’s faithfulness does not depend on human response.

After Paul asks whether there is still value in being a Jew, he immediately answers with a clear “Yes—much in every way.” He does not deny the privilege of belonging to God’s people. Instead, he shows that Israel truly had a special privilege, and one of the greatest is this: they were entrusted with the very words of God.

This means they were the first to receive God’s revelation—His promises, His law, and His message. This is not a small thing. It is a tremendous privilege and responsibility. Through them, the world came to know who God is and what His will is.

But this also introduces tension. If those entrusted with God’s Word were unfaithful to it, what does that mean? Did God fail? Did His plan become meaningless?

Paul’s answer is immediate and strong: “Absolutely not!”

This passage shows a firm truth: God’s faithfulness is never destroyed by human unfaithfulness.

People may fail. Some may ignore God’s Word. Others may even misuse it. But none of these affect who God is.

God remains true—not because of human response, but because of His own nature.

At the same time, the passage also shows that privilege always comes with responsibility. Receiving God’s Word is not only an honor; it is a call to live according to it. The problem is not a lack of revelation, but a lack of response.

God’s faithfulness is most fully revealed in Jesus Christ. Despite repeated human failure, God did not withdraw His promise. Instead, He fulfilled it through Christ.

Christ is the very Word of God made flesh—not only entrusted, but fully revealed. In Him, we see that God’s plan does not depend on human ability to remain faithful, but on His own faithfulness to save.

In our time, we may not be in the same situation as Israel, but we also have been entrusted with God’s Word. There are many ways we can know the truth—through the Bible, teachings, and the community of faith.

But the question remains: what are we doing with what has been entrusted to us?

It is easy to treat God’s Word as mere information—something known but not lived out. It is also easy to become complacent, thinking that “knowing” is enough without real obedience.

But this passage reminds us that the real issue is not how much we know, but how we respond to what we know.

CONCLUSION

God does not change even when people fail. His faithfulness is firm and does not depend on our faithfulness.

But His gifts—especially His Word—are not meant to be ignored. They are entrusted to us so that we may be transformed, obey, and live in truth.

In the end, the question is not whether God is faithful—because He always is.

The question is: how will we respond to that faithfulness?
