Daily Devotional Verses



Romans 5:12-14

NKJV (New King James Version)

Death in Adam, Life in Christ

12 Therefore, just as through one man sin entered the world, and death through sin, and thus death spread to all men, because all sinned— 13 (For until the law sin was in the world, but sin is not imputed when there is no law. 14 Nevertheless death reigned from Adam to Moses, even over those who had not sinned according to the likeness of the transgression of Adam, who is a type of Him who was to come.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

Adam Is a Foreshadowing of the Coming Christ

At the end of this verse, Paul makes a brief yet profoundly important statement.

"Adam is a type of the One who was to come."

The One Paul refers to as "the One who was to come" is the Messiah whom God promised—our Lord Jesus Christ.

What does this mean?

Paul is not saying that Adam and Christ share the same character. In fact, their actions stand in complete contrast. Adam disobeyed God, whereas Christ perfectly obeyed the Father's will.

The word "type" (type or foreshadowing) means that Adam serves as a picture or prophetic pattern pointing forward to the coming of Christ.

Both served as representatives, but the results of their actions were completely different.

Adam became humanity's representative through whom sin entered the world. When he sinned, the consequences of his disobedience extended to all his descendants. Through one man, sin and death entered the world.

Jesus Christ, on the other hand, is the Representative of all who place their faith in Him. Through His perfect obedience to the Father, His death on the cross, and His resurrection, He brought righteousness, grace, and life to all who trust in Him.

With this short statement, Paul introduces one of the most significant comparisons in the entire book of Romans. In the verses that follow, he will show that what Christ accomplished not only reverses the effects of Adam's sin but far surpasses them in greatness and power.

If Adam brought sin into humanity, Christ brought salvation.

If Adam brought condemnation, Christ brought justification.

If Adam became the means through which death entered, Christ became the Giver of life.

Here we see the beauty of God's redemptive plan. He did not allow Adam's sin to have the final word. From the very first fall of humanity, God was already preparing the coming of the Savior who would bring complete victory over sin and death.

CONCLUSION:

It is important to understand that the entire story of the Bible ultimately points to Jesus Christ. Even Adam, the first man, served only as a foreshadowing of the One who is far greater than he is.

If we inherited sin and death through Adam, we receive righteousness and life through Christ. Therefore, our hope does not rest on our identity as descendants of Adam but on our new identity in Jesus Christ.

This is the good news of the gospel. Humanity's fall was not the end of God's plan. From the very beginning, He had already prepared the coming of the Savior who would redeem humanity and bring a hope that neither sin nor death can ever destroy.

Ultimately, the question for each of us is not merely, "Am I a child of Adam?" for by birth we all are. The more important question is, "Do I now belong to Jesus Christ through faith?" For only in Christ can we find grace that is greater than sin, righteousness that we could never achieve on our own, and life that will triumph over death forever.
