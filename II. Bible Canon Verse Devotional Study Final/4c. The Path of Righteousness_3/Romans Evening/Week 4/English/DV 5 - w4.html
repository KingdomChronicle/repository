Daily Devotional Verses



Romans 5:15-17

NKJV (New King James Version)

Death in Adam, Life in Christ

15 But the free gift is not like the offense. For if by the one man’s offense many died, much more the grace of God and the gift by the grace of the one Man, Jesus Christ, abounded to many. 16 And the gift is not like that which came through the one who sinned. For the judgment which came from one offense resulted in condemnation, but the free gift which came from many offenses resulted in justification. 17 For if by the one man’s offense death reigned through the one, much more those who receive abundance of grace and of the gift of righteousness will reign in life through the One, Jesus Christ.)

GUIDE REFLECTION:

The Reign of Death

In the previous part of the passage, Paul showed that Adam's one act of disobedience brought condemnation upon humanity. Now he describes the far-reaching consequence of that judgment. Death did not merely enter the world—it reigned. Through this powerful imagery, Paul portrays the condition of the entire human race after Adam's sin.

“Through the disobedience of one man, death reigned.”

Paul did not say that death simply came or became part of the human experience. He used the image of a king who rules and reigns. From the moment Adam sinned, death became a ruling power over all humanity. No one born from Adam has escaped its dominion.

The reign of death refers not only to the physical death experienced by every person. It also portrays the power of sin, which brought separation between humanity and God and subjected all creation to corruption and suffering. The world that was once created "very good" became a world marked by pain, decay, and death because of one act of disobedience.

This reveals how far-reaching the effects of Adam's sin truly were. He alone was not the only one affected by what he did. As the representative of humanity, his disobedience brought about a reign that extended to every generation. Therefore, no one can claim to be free from the power of death through his own strength.

Many people live as though they have complete control over their lives. Yet Paul reminds us that there is a power that has long reigned over sinful humanity. Death serves as a constant reminder that sin has real consequences and that no one can escape them through personal ability or good works.

This is not merely a truth taught in Scripture. It is also evident throughout the whole history of humanity. For thousands of years, no generation has ceased searching for a way to overcome death. From ancient times to the modern age, mankind has invested enormous amounts of time, intellect, and resources in extending life. Hospitals have been built, scientists have conducted countless studies, doctors and researchers have developed medicines and vaccines, and people continue to invent advanced technologies to fight disease and delay death.

Today, that pursuit has expanded even further. Research continues in areas such as slowing the aging process, regenerative medicine, gene therapy, stem cell therapy, artificial organs, and many other emerging technologies aimed at extending human life.

Yet despite the remarkable advances in science, medicine, and technology, one truth remains unchanged: no human being has ever succeeded in ending the reign of death. Life may be prolonged, many diseases may be cured, and death may be delayed, but it cannot be completely removed. In the end, every person—whether rich or poor, powerful or ordinary, scientist or physician—will still face death.

This is precisely what confirms Paul's statement. Death is not merely a natural event of life but a reigning power over all humanity as the consequence of sin. Humanity's relentless pursuit of conquering death is a quiet admission that there is a power it cannot overcome by its own strength. As human discoveries continue to increase, the limits of human ability become even clearer. No science, medicine, invention, or technology can nullify the judgment brought about by Adam's sin.

In reality, humanity's greatest problem is not merely how to extend life in this world, but how to overcome the death that is the result of sin. And this is a problem that cannot be solved by human wisdom but only by the grace of God through Jesus Christ.

Yet this is not God's final word. While Paul describes the reign of death, he is also preparing our hearts for an even greater truth. If death reigned through one man, what then will be the result of what the one Man, Jesus Christ, has done?

CONCLUSION:

This passage shows that the result of Adam's sin was not merely death but the reign of death over the whole human race. No one has the ability to free himself from its power.

Therefore, we must not place our confidence in our own strength or goodness. Our condition reminds us that we need a Savior whose power is greater than the reign of death.

So let us acknowledge our true condition before God and place our hope in Him. Recognizing the depth of our need prepares our hearts to understand more fully the greatness of what Jesus Christ has accomplished.

Ultimately, this passage shows that Adam's sin gave rise to the reign of death over all humanity. Yet as we acknowledge the power of that death, God is also preparing us to behold the far greater reign of life found only in Jesus Christ.
