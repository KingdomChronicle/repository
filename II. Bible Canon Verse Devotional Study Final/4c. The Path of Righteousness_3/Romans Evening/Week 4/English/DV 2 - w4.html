Daily Devotional Verses



Romans 5:15-17

NKJV (New King James Version)

Death in Adam, Life in Christ

15 But the free gift is not like the offense. For if by the one man’s offense many died, much more the grace of God and the gift by the grace of the one Man, Jesus Christ, abounded to many. 16 And the gift is not like that which came through the one who sinned. For the judgment which came from one offense resulted in condemnation, but the free gift which came from many offenses resulted in justification. 17 For if by the one man’s offense death reigned through the one, much more those who receive abundance of grace and of the gift of righteousness will reign in life through the One, Jesus Christ.)

GUIDE REFLECTION:

The Grace of God Is Far Greater

In the previous part of the passage, Paul revealed the extent of the devastation brought about by Adam's disobedience. Through his sin, many died. But that is not where the story of humanity ends. In the midst of the dark picture of sin, Paul now introduces a truth that brings genuine hope.

“But the grace of God and the free gift by the grace of the one Man, Jesus Christ, abounded to many.”

The word “But” marks a major turning point in Paul's argument. After describing the destructive consequences of Adam's sin, he now points to a far more powerful reality—the grace of God. If sin brought death to many, God's grace through Jesus Christ brought a hope that is far greater than the damage caused by sin.

The grace of God is His freely given love and goodness bestowed upon those who are undeserving to receive it. It is not the result of our goodness, our efforts, or our worthiness. It is a free gift that flows from the very heart of God. Salvation, therefore, does not begin with man but with God, who first extended His grace.

But why does Paul say that the grace of God is far greater than the effects of Adam's sin? It is not because sin was insignificant. On the contrary, its devastation was immeasurable. Through one act of disobedience, the whole human race came under sin, death, and the judgment of God. Humanity no longer possessed the ability to save itself or restore its broken relationship with God.

Yet what Jesus Christ accomplished did far more than merely recover what was lost through Adam. His grace brought blessings that surpass what humanity had lost. God not only forgave us but also declared us righteous through Christ. He not only rescued us from condemnation but also received us as His own children. As the Scripture says, “For both He who sanctifies and those who are being sanctified are all of one, for which reason He is not ashamed to call them brethren.” (Hebrews 2:11). Those who were once sinners and enemies of God have now been welcomed into His family and are called brothers and sisters of His own Son.

The grace of God does not end there. The salvation we have received today will be fully revealed at Christ's return. The day is coming when our bodies, now subject to weakness and death, will be transformed into glorified bodies like His glorious body. The present creation, now under the curse, will give way to a new heaven and a new earth where there will be no more sin, suffering, or death.

Even the purpose God originally gave to humanity will be fulfilled even more gloriously in Christ. In the beginning, Adam was placed to rule over the physical creation as God's representative. But he failed because of sin. Through Jesus Christ, however, the redeemed are not merely restored to their former condition. As fellow heirs with Christ, they will share in His eternal reign. Thus, the grace of God does not simply restore what was lost through Adam; it brings His people into a far greater position through union with Christ.

This is why Paul declares that the grace of God is far greater. Adam's sin brought death and separation from God, but Christ's grace brings forgiveness, righteousness, a new identity as children of God, and the sure hope of living and reigning with Him forever. God's grace is not merely sufficient to remove the consequences of sin; it is abundantly greater than all the destruction sin has caused.

When we truly understand the greatness of this grace, our pride disappears. We have nothing to boast about before God, for our salvation, our new life, and our eternal hope all come solely from His grace. Therefore, the only fitting response of our hearts is humble faith, sincere gratitude, and continual trust in Jesus Christ.

CONCLUSION:

This passage reveals that God's response to humanity's sin is not merely judgment, but grace. Although the damage caused by Adam's disobedience was immense, God's free gift through Jesus Christ is infinitely greater.

In Him, the condemnation of sin has not only been removed. We have been forgiven, declared righteous, received as God's children, and given the assurance of a glorious future with Him. This demonstrates that the grace of God is truly greater than all the consequences of sin.

Therefore, we must not place our confidence in our own goodness or ability. Our hope begins and ends with the grace of God freely given through Jesus Christ. As we live in this world, may we continually trust Him, worship Him, and live lives marked by grateful hearts.

In the end, the grace of God did far more than simply remove the damage caused by sin. Through Jesus Christ, He has brought His redeemed people into a greater hope, a deeper relationship with Him, and an eternal glory far more abundant than everything that was lost because of Adam's transgression.
