Daily Devotional Verses



Proverbs 10:19

NKJV (New King James Version)

The Proverbs of Solomon:

In the multitude of words sin is not lacking, But he who restrains his lips is wise.

GUIDE REFLECTION:

Wisdom Knows When to Speak and When to Remain Silent

One of the most difficult parts of our body to control is the tongue. Often, we get ourselves into trouble not because of what we have done, but because of the words we have spoken.

That is why Solomon said:

"When words are many, sin is not absent, but whoever restrains the tongue is wise."

This verse is not saying that speaking is wrong or that we should always remain silent.

Solomon is referring to the person who has no restraint in speech—someone who always has something to say, even when it is no longer necessary.

When we do not carefully consider our words, we are more likely to say something wrong.

👉 Not everything that comes to mind needs to be spoken. One mark of wisdom is knowing when to speak and when to remain silent.

Many Sins Begin with the Mouth

When we speak excessively, various kinds of sin can easily enter in.

It may lead to:

  • gossip,
  • slander,
  • lying,
  • rash promises,
  • hurtful words,
  • boasting,
  • or meaningless conversation.

A word spoken in anger can destroy a relationship.

A false statement can wound another person's heart.

A piece of gossip can ruin someone's reputation.

It is easy to speak, but difficult to take back words once they have left our mouths.

👉 A spoken word is like an arrow that has been released—once it is gone, it cannot be brought back.

A Picture of the Power of Words

There is a well-known story about a man who came to a servant of God seeking counsel. He admitted that he had shared a story about his friend even though he was not certain whether what he had heard was true. He feared that he might have spread gossip and hurt another person.

Instead of immediately scolding him, the servant of God instructed him to write on a piece of paper. Then he said, "Tear it into many tiny pieces, and as you go to work tomorrow, scatter them along the road."

The man did as he was instructed.

The following day, the servant of God said, "Now go back along the road you traveled and gather every piece of paper again."

The man shook his head.

"How could I possibly do that? Some have already been carried away by the wind, others have been stepped on, and still others have ended up in the gutter or the trash."

The servant of God replied:

"That is what our words are like. Once they leave our mouths, we can never fully take them back. We may ask for forgiveness, but the words we have scattered may continue to cause pain, doubt, and damage to another person's reputation."

The same is true of gossip, slander, stories that have not first been verified, and even harsh outbursts. They may leave our mouths in only a few moments, but their effects may last for many years.

Before we speak about another person, it is wise to ask ourselves:

  • Is it true?
  • Am I certain?
  • Will what I am about to say be beneficial?
  • Does it really need to be said?

If not, it is better to remain silent than to become an instrument of falsehood, hurt someone's feelings, or damage another person's dignity.

A wise person does not merely ask whether he has something to say, but whether it truly ought to be said.

👉 Wisdom is not measured by how much we know or by the length of what we say, but by our ability to choose words that are truly necessary, helpful, and glorifying to God.

The Tongue Is Small Yet Powerful

In the New Testament, James expands on this teaching.

He describes the tongue as a small part of the body, yet one with great power.

Just as a small fire can burn a vast forest, one wrong word can cause great turmoil.

That is why he said:

"Be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath." (James 1:19)

God gave human beings two ears but only one mouth.

Although this is not the primary purpose of how we were created, it is a good reminder that we should listen more than we speak.

👉 A person who knows how to listen learns more than one who is always speaking.

Words Are a Mirror of the Heart

When our speech is wrong, the problem is not only with the mouth.

Its root goes much deeper.

Jesus taught that what comes out of the mouth comes from the heart.

If the heart is filled with anger, that anger will come out in our speech.

If our hearts are filled with envy, bitterness, or pride, these will also be evident in our words.

On the other hand, if the heart is filled with love, grace, and truth, our speech will also be good.

Therefore, the real solution is not merely to restrain our mouths, but to allow God to transform our hearts.

👉 Our speech is a reflection of the condition of our hearts.

Use Your Words to Give Life

We are not called merely to avoid harmful words.

We are also called to use our speech for the good of others.

Paul taught:

"Let no corrupt word proceed out of your mouth, but what is good for necessary edification, that it may impart grace to the hearers." (Ephesians 4:29)

Every day, we are given an opportunity to choose.

We can speak words that:

  • bring hope,
  • encourage others,
  • teach the truth,
  • lead people to Christ,
  • and reveal the love of God.

Or we can use our mouths to wound, destroy, and spread evil.

👉 The same mouth can become an instrument of blessing or destruction. Let us choose to use it for the glory of God.

Living It Out: What Comes Out of My Mouth?

Examine yourself.

  • Do I speak too quickly without thinking first?
  • Do my words strengthen others or hurt them?
  • Do I know how to remain silent when I am angry?
  • Am I better known as a good listener than as someone who talks too much?
  • When I speak, do people see the love and wisdom of Christ?

Let us ask the Lord for the wisdom to control our speech and cleanse our hearts, for that is where everything we say begins.

CONCLUSION

Proverbs 10:19 teaches that wisdom is not measured by how much we say, but by the quality of our words and our ability to exercise restraint when necessary.

In a world filled with noise, opinions, and endless conversation, a person who knows how to listen, think, and speak at the right time demonstrates true wisdom. His words do not simply flow from emotional impulses, but from a heart being shaped by God.

Above all, let us remember that our speech is a mirror of our hearts. If we want our words to become a fountain of life, we must allow Christ to transform our hearts each day.

The question:

When people hear your words, do they experience God's grace, truth, and hope, or are they wounded by words that your tongue failed to restrain?
