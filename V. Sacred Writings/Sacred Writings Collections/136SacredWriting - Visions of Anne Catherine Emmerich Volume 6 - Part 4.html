From the Second Feast of Tabernacles to the First Conversion of the Magdalene

Chapter 1

The Pagan Cyrinus of Cyprus

1 From the school Jesus went in the evening twilight, accompanied by the disciples and the people who had awaited Him outside the synagogue, up to Thabor.
2 A multitude of others and some of His own relatives were already there assembled.
3 Jesus sat down on the mountain, His hearers reclining or sitting below at His feet.
4 The stars were twinkling in the sky, and the moon was shedding around her gentle radiance.
5 Jesus taught until late into the night.
6 He often did this even after a toilsome day’s work when in the midst of a little band of pious souls.
7 The peace was then more profound, His audience less distracted; the heavens, the stars, the wide expanse of nature, the pleasant coolness of the air, the stillness reigning around, fell like soothing balm upon men’s souls.
8 They heard their Teacher’s voice more distinctly, comprehended His words more easily, were less confused at hearing their own faults laid bare, carried His instructions home with them, and pondered them with fewer distractions.
9 This was especially the case in the magnificent region in which Jesus now was, in the wide prospect that unfolded from the heights of Thabor.
10 The mount itself, on account of the sojourn of Elias and Malachias upon it for a time, was held in special veneration.
11 When Jesus was returning home late in the night, followed by the crowd, there approached Him on the way a pagan from Cyprus who had been present at the instructions.
12 He was one of the occupants of Jesse’s house and had something to do connected with the manufacture of the essential oils.
13 Up to this time, however, he had kept aloof through a spirit of humility.
14 But now Jesus took him into a room of the house where He sat with him alone, as He had done with Nicodemus, instructing him and answering the questions that he put so humbly, yet with so eager a desire of learning the truth.
15 This pagan, a man most noble and wise, was named Cyrinus.
16 His remarks were most profound, and he received Jesus’ instructions with indescribable humility and joy.
17 Jesus, on His side, was very loving and confidential toward him.
18 Cyrinus said that for a long time past he had been sensible of the emptiness of idolatry and had longed to become a Jew, but that there was one thing which presented an insuperable objection, namely, circumcision.
19 He asked whether it was not possible to attain salvation without it.
20 Jesus answered him in words both familiar and significant regarding that mystery.
21 He might, said Jesus, circumcise his senses, his heart, and his tongue from carnal desires and pleasures, and then go to Capharnaum for Baptism.
22 At these words, Cyrinus asked why He did not preach that openly, for he thought that if Jesus did so, many pagans who were longing for it would be converted.
23 Jesus answered that if He should say such a thing to the multitude, blinded as they were by their prejudices, they would certainly put Him to death, and one must not scandalize the weak.
24 Again, abolishing circumcision might give rise to multiplied sects; besides, the law was necessary for some of the pagans as a means of trial and sacrifice.
25 But now that the Kingdom of God was drawing near, the covenant of circumcision in the flesh was fulfilled and the circumcision of the heart and the spirit must take its place.
26 Cyrinus inquired also as to the sufficiency of John’s baptism, and Jesus spoke with him upon that point.
27 He told Jesus about many people who were sighing after Him in Cyprus, and complained to Him of his two sons who, though otherwise very virtuous, were fierce enemies of Judaism.
28 Jesus consoled him and promised that, after He had fulfilled His mission, his sons would yet become zealous workers in His vineyard.
29 These sons were, I think, called Aristarchus and Trophimus.
0 They afterward became disciples of the Apostles.
1 This most touching nocturnal interview lasted till morning.
2 On the sunny side of the mountain were large reservoirs hewn out of the rocky wall, and in them were vessels belonging to Jesse, in which were prepared perfumes from herbs and other substances.
3 The oil dropped from one vessel into another, making many a turn in its course.


Chapter 2

Jesus Goes to Giskala, The Birthplace of St. Paul

1 From Dabereth Jesus went in the forenoon with the disciples three hours northward to the plain and city of Giskala, almost an hour from Bethulia.
2 Just at the outset of His journey lay a place to the east, I think Japhia, and another directly opposite toward the west and northward from Thabor.
3 Giskala was situated upon a height, but one not so elevated as that of Bethulia.
4 It was a stronghold garrisoned by pagan soldiers in Herod’s pay.
5 The Jews dwelt in a little quarter apart, about fifteen minutes distant from the fortress.
6 Giskala was very different from other cities.
7 There were open squares and large buildings surrounded with palisades, as if to afford space for hitching horses, and all around the city ran a wall with towers, from whose stories troops of soldiers could defend it.
8 All this gave Giskala a very remarkable appearance.
9 Near one of the towers stood the idolatrous temple.
10 The Jews of the little city lived on good terms with the pagan soldiers, for whom they manufactured articles of leather, harness for the horses and military equipment for the men.
11 They were likewise partly the owners and partly the overseers and stewards of the fertile region lying around the city.
12 Far from it, off to Capharnaum, stretched the magnificent country of Genesareth.
13 The citadel stood upon a height up to which led a paved road from terrace to terrace.
14 The little Jewish quarter lay outstretched on the declivity of that same height.
15 Before it was a well, or rather a cistern, for drinking water, which was conducted from distant sources by means of pipes.
16 It was by this cistern that Jesus and the disciples sat down on their arrival.
17 The residents of the Jewish quarter were just then celebrating a feast and all the inhabitants, young and old, were out in the gardens and fields.
18 The pagan children from the city were present also, but they kept to themselves somewhat apart from the others.
19 When the people spied Jesus going to the cistern, the chief men of the city, with their learned schoolmaster, approached Him.
20 They welcomed Him and the disciples, washed their feet, and presented them fruit, Jesus, still at the cistern, gave an instruction in which He alluded to the harvest in a parable, for in this region at that moment they were busy gathering in their second harvest of grapes and all kinds of fruit.
21 He next went over to where the pagan children were, spoke to the mothers, blessed them, and cured several who were sick.
22 The Jews of Giskala were on that day celebrating a feast commemorative of their deliverance from the yoke of a tyrant, the first founder of the Sadducees.
23 He lived over two hundred years before Christ, but I have forgotten his name.
24 He was one of the officers of the Sanhedrin in Jerusalem, and was charged to watch over the points of faith not found set down in the written Law.
25 He had tormented the people horribly with his rigorous ideas, one of which was that no reward could be hoped from God, but that He was to be served by them as slaves serve their master.
26 Giskala was his birthplace, but his townsmen held his memory in horror.
27 Today’s festival was a memorial rejoicing at his death.
28 One of his disciples was from Samaria.
29 Sadoch, who denied the dogma of the resurrection of the body, continued to promulgate the founder’s doctrine.
30 He was a pupil of Antigonus.
31 Sadoch also had a Samaritan accomplice helping to propagate his errors.
32 Jesus and His disciples lodged with the Elder of the synagogue, and taught in the forecourt of the same.
33 They brought some sick to Him, whom He healed, among them a dropsical old woman.
34 This Elder of the synagogue was a very good and learned man.
35 The people abhorred the Pharisees and Sadducees, and had taken great care to provide themselves with such a teacher.
36 That he might acquire more knowledge, they had sent him travelling far away, even down to Egypt.
37 Jesus conversed a long time with him.
38 As usual, the Elder turned the conversation upon John, whom he praised very highly.
39 He asked Jesus why, powerful and enlightened as He was said to be and as He was in reality, He did not make some effort to free that man so truly grand and admirable.
40 During His instruction in the forecourt of the synagogue Jesus uttered prophetic words to the disciples concerning Giskala.
41 They were as follows: Three zealots had arisen in Giskala.
42 The first was that one in whose memory the Jews were then celebrating a feast; the second was a great villain, John of Giskala, who had raised a terrible insurrection in Galilee and at the siege of Jerusalem had committed frightful excesses; the third was living at the very time He was speaking.
43 He would pass from hatred to love, would be zealous for the truth, and would convert many to God.
44 This third was Paul, who was born at Giskala, but whose parents afterward removed to Tarsus.
45 After his conversion and when journeying to Jerusalem, Paul very zealously preached the Gospel at Giskala.
46 His parents’ house was still standing, and rented to strangers.
47 It was situated at the extremity of the suburb of Giskala, and at some distance were squares surrounded with palisades and little buildings, like bleaching huts, that reached almost to the city itself.
48 Paul’s parents must have carried on the manufacture of linen, or perhaps they had a weaver’s establishment.
49 A pagan officer named Achias now rented and lived in the dwelling house.


Chapter 3

Cure of the Son of a Pagan Officer

1 It would be difficult to describe the fruitfulness of the region around Giskala.
2 The people were now gathering the second crop of grapes, different kinds of fruits, aromatic shrubs, and cotton.
3 A kind of reed grew in these parts, the lower leaves of which were large, the upper ones small.
4 From it distilled a sweet juice like resin.
5 Here, too, were seen those trees whose fruit was used for the decoration of the tabernacles.
6 The fruit was called the apples of the Patriarchs, from the fact of their having been brought hither from the warm eastern countries by the Patriarchs.
7 These trees were trained against walls forming an espalier, although their trunk was often more than a foot in diameter.
8 Here also were found many plants producing cotton, whole fields of sweet-scented shrubs, and the aromatic herb from which nard is made.
9 Figs, olives, and grapes were in abundance, while magnificent melons lay in countless numbers in the fields, the roads to which were lined with palms and date trees.
10 In the midst of this luxuriance of nature were great herds of cattle grazing in the most beautiful meadows covered with grass and herbs.
11 I saw likewise large trees with great, thick nuts, the wood of which was exceedingly tough and solid.
12 As Jesus was walking through the fields and gardens into which the people were fast gathering, groups collected around Him here and there.
13 He instructed them in parables taken from their ordinary circumstances and occupations.
14 The pagan children mingled familiarly with those of their Jewish neighbors in harvest time, but they were somewhat differently clothed.
15 In the house in which Paul was born there lived at this period an officer in command of the pagan soldiers of the citadel.
16 He was called Achias.
17 He had a sick son seven years old, to whom he had given the name of Jephte after the Jewish hero.
18 Achias was a good man.
19 He sighed for help from Jesus, but none of the inhabitants of Giskala would intercede for him with the Lord.
20 The disciples were all engaged: some busy around their Master, others scattered among the harvesters to whom they were telling of Jesus and repeating His instructions, while some others had already been dispatched as messengers to Capharnaum and into the neighboring districts.
21 The townspeople had no liking for the officer, whom they did not care to have so near them.
22 They would have been glad had he fixed his abode elsewhere.
23 They were, besides, not very friendly in disposition, and even showed very little enthusiasm over Jesus Himself.
24 They went carelessly on with their work, listening to His words, but taking no lively, active interest.
25 The anxious father therefore made bold to follow Jesus, but at a distance.
26 At last he approached Him, stepped before Him, bowing, and said: “Master, reject not Thy servant! Have pity on my little son lying sick at home!” Jesus replied: “It behooveth to break bread to the children of the household before giving it to the stranger who stands without.
27 Achias responded: “Lord, I believe the Promise.
28 I believe that Thou hast said that such as believe in Thee are not strangers but Thy children.
29 Lord, have pity on my son!” Then said Jesus: “Thy faith hath saved thee!” and followed by some of the disciples, He went into the house in which Paul was born and in which Achias now resided.
30 It was rather more elegant than the generality of Jewish dwellings, though its arrangements were pretty much of the same style.
31 There was a courtyard in front, from which one entered a broad hall, on either side of which were sleeping apartments, or spaces, cut off from the main portion by movable screens.
32 In the center of the house arose the fireplace.
33 Around it lay large rooms and halls, provided with broad stone benches near the walls, upon which lay rugs and cushions.
34 The windows were high up in the building.
35 Achias conducted Jesus into the interior of the house, and some of the servants carried to Him the boy in his bed.
36 The wife of Achias followed veiled.
37 She bowed timidly, and stood somewhat behind the rest in anxious expectation.
38 Achias was radiant with joy.
39 He called in all his domestics who, full of curiosity, were standing at a distance.
40 The boy was a beautiful child of about six years.
41 He had on a long woolen gown and a striped fur around his neck and crossed on the breast.
42 He was dumb and paralyzed, wholly unable to move.
43 But he looked intelligent and affectionate, and cast upon Jesus a most touching glance.
44 Jesus addressed to the parents and all present some words on the vocation of the Gentiles, the nearness of the Kingdom, of penance, and of the entrance into the Father’s house by Baptism.
45 Then He prayed, took the boy from his little bed up in His arms, laid him on His breast, bowed low over him, put His fingers under his tongue, set him down on the floor, and led him to the officer who, with the mother trembling for joy, rushed forward with heartfelt tears to meet and embrace their child.
46 The little fellow, likewise stretching out his arms toward his parents, cried: “O father! O mother! I can walk, I can again speak!” Then Jesus said: “Take the boy! Ye know not what a treasure has been given to you in him.
47 He is now restored to you, but he will one day be redemanded of you!” The parents led the child again to Jesus and in tears threw themselves with him at His feet, uttering thanks.
48 Jesus blessed the boy and spoke to him most kindly.
49 The officer begged Jesus to step with him into an adjoining apartment and take some refreshment.
50 This He did along with the disciples.
51 They partook, standing, of bread, honey, small fruits, and some kind of beverage.
52 Jesus again spoke with Achias, telling him that he should go to Capharnaum and there receive Baptism, and that he might join Zorobabel.
53 Achias and his domestics did this later on.
54 The boy Jephte afterward became a very zealous disciple of St. Thomas.
55 The soldiers of Giskala, in quality of guards, assisted at the Crucifixion of Christ.
56 They were on similar occasions employed as police.
57 Jesus bade farewell to the home of the happy Achias.
58 He spoke with His disciples of the child and of the fruits of salvation he was destined to reap.
59 He told them also that from that same house one had already gone out who would accomplish great things in His Kingdom.


Chapter 4

Jesus Teaches in Gabara. Magdalen’s First Conversion

1 On leaving Giskala, Jesus did not go to Bethulia, which was near, but leaving it on the left, He traversed the valley and the plain to the somewhat important city of Gabara.
2 It lay at the western foot of the mountain on whose south-eastern slope was perched the Herodian eyrie Jetebatha.
3 The distance between the city and the fortress, that is, if one went around the mountain, was one hour.
4 This mountain, in which steps were hewn, arose like a steep wall behind Gabara, whose inhabitants were engaged in the manufacture of cotton fine as silk, which they wove into cloth and covers.
5 They made of it also a kind of mattress, which they stretched and fastened on hooks.
6 This formed the whole bed.
7 Some others were engaged in salting and exporting fish.
8 While still in Giskala, Jesus had sent some of the disciples around to the neighboring places to say that He would deliver a great instruction on the mountain beyond Gabara.
9 There came in consequence, from a circuit of several hours, large crowds of people, who encamped around the mountain.
10 On the summit was an enclosed space in which was a teacher’s chair long out of use.
11 Peter, Andrew, James, John, Nathanael Chased, and all the rest of the disciples had come, besides most of John’s disciples and the sons of the Blessed Virgin’s eldest sister.
12 There were altogether about sixty disciples, friends, and relatives of Jesus here assembled.
13 The more intimate of the disciples were greeted by Jesus with clasping of both hands and pressing cheek to cheek.
14 Crowds of heathens came from Cydessa, one hour westward of the neighboring city of Damna, from Adama and the country around Lake Merom.
15 The people crowding hither brought with them provisions and sick of all kinds.
16 Cydessa was a heathen city in the heart of Zabulon.
17 It was in ruins in the time of Alexander the Great, who bestowed it upon a man from Tyre called Livias.
18 The latter restored it, and led thither many of his pagan countrymen from Tyre.
19 The first pagans that came to John’s baptism were from Cydessa, which was very beautifully situated and commanded a view of the luxuriantly fruitful country around.
20 Magdalen also wended her way to the mount of instruction near Gabara.
21 Martha and Anna Cleophas had left Damna, where the holy women had an inn, and gone to Magdalum with the view of persuading Magdalen to attend the sermon that Jesus was about to deliver on the mountain beyond Gabara.
22 Veronica, Johanna Chusa, Dina, and the Suphanite had meanwhile remained at Damna, distant three hours from Capharnaum and over one hour from Magdalum.
23 Magdalen received her sister in a manner rather kind and showed her into an apartment not far from her room of state, but into this latter she did not take her.
24 There was in Magdalen a mixture of true and false shame.
25 She was partly ashamed of her simple, pious, and plainly dressed sister who went around with Jesus’ followers so despised by her visitors and associates, and she was partly ashamed of herself before Martha.
26 It was this feeling that prevented her taking the latter into the apartments that were the scenes of her follies and vices.
27 Magdalen was somewhat broken in spirits, but she lacked the courage to disengage herself from her surroundings.
28 She looked pale and languid.
29 The man with whom she lived, on account of his low and vulgar sentiments, was utterly distasteful to her.
30 Martha treated her very prudently and affectionately.
31 She said to her: “Dina and Mary, the Suphanite, whom you know, two amiable and clever women, invite you to be present with them at the instruction that Jesus is going to give on the mountain.
32 It is so near, and they are so anxious for your company.
33 You need not be ashamed of them before the people, for they are respectable, they dress with taste, and they have distinguished manners.
34 You will behold a very wonderful spectacle: the crowds of people, the marvelous eloquence of the Prophet, the sick, the cures that He effects, the hardihood with which He addresses the Pharisees! Veronica, Mary Chusa, and Jesus’ Mother, who wishes you so well—we all are convinced that you will thank us for the invitation.
35 I think it will cheer you up a little.
36 You appear to be quite forlorn here, you have no one around you who can appreciate your heart and your talents.
37 Oh, if you would only pass some time with us in Bethania! We hear so many wonderful things, and we have so much good to do, and you have always been so full of compassion and kindness.
38 You must at least come to Damna with me tomorrow morning.
39 There you will find all the women of our party at the inn.
40 You can have a private apartment and meet only those that you know,” etc.
41 In this strain Martha spoke to her sister, carefully avoiding anything that might wound her.
42 Magdalen’s sadness predisposed her to listen favorably to Martha’s proposals.
43 She did indeed raise a few difficulties, but at last yielded and promised Martha to accompany her to Damna.
44 She took a repast with her and went several times during the evening from her own apartments to see her.
45 Martha and Anna Cleophas prayed together that night that God would render the coming journey fruitful in good for Magdalen.
46 A few days previously James the Greater, impelled by a feeling of intense compassion for Magdalen, had come to invite her to the preaching soon to take place at Gabara.
47 She had received him at a neighboring house.
48 James was in appearance very imposing.
49 His speech was grave and full of wisdom, though at the same time most pleasing.
50 He made a most favorable impression upon Magdalen, and she received him graciously whenever he was in that part of the country.
51 James did not address to her words of reproof; on the contrary, his manner toward her was marked by esteem and kindliness, and he invited her to be present at least once at Jesus’ preaching.
52 It would be impossible, he said, to see or hear one superior to Him.
53 She had no need to trouble herself about the other auditors, and she might appear among them in her ordinary dress.
54 Magdalen had received his invitation favorably, but she was still undecided as to whether she should or should not accept it, when Martha and Anna Cleophas arrived.
55 On the eve of the day appointed for the instruction, Magdalen with Martha and Anna Cleophas started from Magdalum to join the holy women at Damna.
56 Magdalen rode on an ass, for she was not accustomed to walking.
57 She was dressed elegantly, though not to such excess nor so extravagantly as at a later period when she was converted for the second time.
58 She took a private apartment in the inn and spoke only with Dina and the Suphanite, who visited her by turns.
59 I saw them together, an affable and well-bred confidence marking their intercourse.
60 There was, however, on the part of the converted sinners, a shade of embarrassment similar to what might be experienced on a military officer’s meeting a former comrade who had become a priest.
61 This feeling soon gave way to tears and womanly expressions of mutual sympathy, and they went together to the inn at the foot of the mountain.
62 The other holy women did not go to the instruction, in order not to annoy Magdalen by their presence.
63 They had come to Damna with the intention of prevailing upon Jesus to remain there and not go to Capharnaum where Pharisees from various localities were again assembled.
64 They, the Pharisees, had taken up their abode together, determined to make Capharnaum their headquarters for awhile, since it was the central point of all Jesus’ journeyings.
65 The young Pharisee from Samaria who was present the last time was not among this set; another had taken his place.
66 At Nazareth also and in other places the Pharisees had formed similar unions against Jesus.
67 The holy women, and especially Mary, were very much troubled, for the Pharisees had uttered loud threats.
68 They sent a messenger to Jesus imploring Him not to go to Capharnaum after this instruction, but to join them in Damna; or He might turn to the right or to the left as seemed good to Him; or better perhaps would it be for Him to cross the lake and preach among the pagan cities where He would run no risk.
69 Jesus replied by sending them word not to worry about Him, that He knew what was best for Him to do, and that He would see them again in Capharnaum.
70 The Mount of Instruction near Gabara.
71 Magdalen Magdalen and her companions reached the mountain in good time, and found crowds of people already encamped around it.
72 The sick of all kinds were, according to the nature of their maladies, ranged together in different places under light canopies and arbors.
73 High upon the mountain were the disciples, kindly ranging the people in order and rendering them every assistance.
74 Around the teacher’s chair was a low, semicircular wall, and over it an awning.
75 The audience had here and there similar awnings erected.
76 At a short distance from the teacher’s chair, Magdalen and the other women had found a comfortable seat upon a little eminence.
77 About ten o’clock, Jesus ascended the mountain with His disciples, followed by the Pharisees, the Herodians, and the Sadducees, and took the teacher’s chair.
78 The disciples were on one side, the Pharisees on the other, forming a circle around Him.
79 Several times during His discourse, Jesus made a pause to allow His hearers to exchange places, the more distant coming forward, the nearest falling back, and He likewise repeated the same instructions several times.
80 His auditors partook of refreshments in the intervals, and Jesus Himself once took a mouthful to eat and a little drink.
81 This discourse of Jesus was one of the most powerful that He had yet delivered.
82 He prayed before He began, and then told His hearers that they should not be scandalized at Him if He called God His Father, for whosoever does the will of the Father in Heaven, he is His son, and that He really accomplished the Father’s will, He clearly proved.
83 Hereupon He prayed aloud to His Father and then commenced His austere preaching of penance after the manner of the ancient Prophets.
84 All that had happened from the time of the first Promise, all the figures and all the menaces, He introduced into His discourse and showed how, in the present and in the near future, they would be accomplished.
85 He proved the coming of the Messiah from the fulfillment of the Prophecies.
86 He spoke of John, the precursor and preparer of the ways, who had honestly fulfilled his mission, but whose hearers had remained obdurate.
87 Then He enumerated their vices, their hypocrisy, their idolatry of sinful flesh; painted in strong colors the Pharisees, Sadducees, and Herodians; and spoke with great warmth of the anger of God and the approaching judgment, of the destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple, and of the diverse woes that hung over their country.
88 He quoted many passages from the prophet Malachias, explaining and applying them to the Precursor, to the Messiah, to the pure oblation of bread and wine of the New Law (which I plainly understood to signify the Holy Sacrifice of the Mass), to the judgment awaiting the godless, to the second coming of the Messiah on the last day, and spoke of the confidence and consolation those that feared God would then experience.
89 He added, moreover, that the grace taken from them would be given to the heathens.
90 Then turning to the disciples, Jesus exhorted them to confidence and perseverance, and told them that He would send them to preach salvation to all nations.
91 He warned them to hold neither to the Pharisees, the Sadducees, nor the Herodians, whom He painted in lively colors by comparisons as just as they were striking.
92 This was peculiarly vexatious to the last named, since no one wanted to be publicly known as an Herodian.
93 They who adhered to this sect did so mostly in secret.
94 When in the course of His instruction Jesus observed that if His hearers would not accept the salvation offered them, it would be worse for them than for Sodom and Gomorrha, some of the Pharisees, taking advantage of a pause, stepped up to Him with the question: “Then, will this mountain, this city, yes, even the whole country, be swallowed up along with us all? And could there happen something still worse?” Jesus answered: “The stones of Sodom were swallowed up, but not all the souls, for these latter knew not of the Promise, nor had they the Law and the prophets.
95 He added some words that I understood of His own future descent into Limbo, and from which I gathered that many of those souls were saved.
96 Then coming back to the Jews of His own time, He reminded them that they were a chosen race whom God had formed into one nation, that they had received instruction and warnings, the Promises and their realization, that if they rejected them and persevered in their incredulity, not the rocks, the mountains (for they obeyed the Lord), but their own stony hearts, their own souls, would be hurled into the abyss.
97 And thus would their lot be more grievous than that of Sodom.
98 When Jesus had, thus vehemently urged the guilty to penance, when He had so severely pronounced judgment upon the obdurate, He became once more all love, invited all sinners to come to Him, and even shed over them tears of compassion.
99 Then He implored His Father to touch their hearts that some, a few, yes, even one, though burdened with all kinds of guilt, might return to Him.
100 Could He gain but one soul, He would share all with it, He would give all that He possessed, yes, He would even sacrifice His life to purchase it.
101 He stretched out His arms toward them, exclaiming: “Come! Come to Me, ye who are weary and laden with guilt! Come to Me, ye sinners! Do penance, believe, and share the Kingdom with Me!” Then turning to the Pharisees, to His enemies, He opened His arms to them also, beseeching all, at least one of them, to come to Him.
102 Magdalen had taken her seat among the other women with the self-confident air of a lady of the world, but her manner was assumed.
103 She was inwardly confused and a prey to interior struggle.
104 At first she gazed around upon the crowd, but when Jesus appeared and began to speak, her eyes and soul were riveted upon Him alone.
105 His exhortations to penance, His lively pictures of vice, His threats of chastisement, affected her powerfully, and unable to suppress her emotions, she trembled and wept beneath her veil.
106 When Jesus, Himself shedding tears full of loving compassion, cried out for sinners to come to Him, many of His hearers were transported with emotion.
107 There was a movement in the circle and the crowd pressed around Him.
108 Magdalen also, and following her example the other women likewise, took a step nearer.
109 But when Jesus exclaimed: “Ah! If even soul would come to Me!” Magdalen was so moved that she wanted to fly to Him at once.
110 She stepped forward; but her companions, fearing some disturbance, held her back, whispering: “Wait! Wait!” This movement of Magdalen attracted scarcely any notice among the bystanders, since the attention of all was riveted upon Jesus’ words.
111 Jesus, aware of Magdalen’s agitation, uttered words of consolation meant only for her.
112 He said: “If even one germ of penance, of contrition, of love, of faith, of hope has, in consequence of My words, fallen upon some poor, erring heart, it will bear fruit, it will be set down in favor of that poor sinner, it will live and increase.
113 I Myself shall nourish it, shall cultivate it, shall present it to My Father.
114 These words consoled Magdalen while they pierced her inmost soul, and she stepped back again among her companions.
115 It was now about six o’clock, and the sun had already sunk low behind the mountain.
116 During His discourse Jesus was turned to the west, the point toward which the teacher’s chair faced, and there was no one behind Him.
117 And now He prayed, dismissed the multitude with His blessing, and commanded the disciples to buy food and distribute it to the poor and needy.
118 Whoever had more than enough for himself was to give it or sell it for the benefit of the poor, who were to take home with them whatever they received over and above.
119 Some of the disciples went immediately to execute their Master’s commission.
120 Most of those present gave willingly what they could spare, while others just as willingly took some indemnification for it.
121 The disciples were well known in this part of the country, so the poor were well cared for, and they thanked the great charity of the Lord.
122 Meanwhile the other disciples accompanied Jesus to the sick, numbers of whom had been brought thither.
123 The Pharisees, scandalized, impressed, astonished, enraged, went back to Gabara.
124 Simon Zabulon, the chief of the synagogue, reminded Jesus of the invitation to sup in his house.
125 Jesus replied that He would be there.
126 The Pharisees murmured against Jesus and criticized Him the whole way down the mountain, finding fault with His doctrine and His manners.
127 Each was ashamed to allow his neighbor to remark the favorable impression that had been made upon him, and so by the time they reached the city, they had again entrenched themselves in their own self-righteousness.
128 Magdalen and her companions followed Jesus.
129 The former went among the people and took her place near the sick women as if to render them assistance.
130 She was very much impressed, and the misery that she witnessed moved her still more.
131 Jesus turned first to the men, among whom for a long time He healed diseases of all kinds.
132 The hymns of thanksgiving from the cured and their attendants as they moved away, rang on the breeze.
133 When He approached the sick females, the crowd that pressed around Him and the need that He and His disciples had of space forced Magdalen and the holy women to fall back a little.
134 Nevertheless, Magdalen sought by every opportunity, by every break in the crowd, to draw near to Him, but Jesus constantly turned away from her.
135 He healed some women afflicted with a flow of blood.
136 But how express the feelings of Magdalen, so delicate, so effeminate, whose eyes were quite unused to the sight of human suffering! What memories, what gratitude swelled the heart of Mary Suphan when six women, bound three and three, were forcibly led to Jesus by strong servant maids who dragged them along with cords, or long linen bands! They were possessed in the most frightful manner by unclean spirits, and they were the first possessed women that I saw brought publicly to Jesus.
137 Some were from beyond the Lake of Genesareth, some from Samaria, and among them were several pagans.
138 They had been bound together only upon reaching this place.
139 Ordinarily they were perfectly quiet and gentle, they offered no violence to one another.
140 But anon, they became quite furious, screaming and hurling themselves here and there.
141 Their custodians bound them and kept them at a distance during Jesus’ discourse, and now when all was nearly over, they brought them forward.
142 As the afflicted creatures drew near to Jesus and the disciples, they began to offer vehement resistance.
143 Satan was tormenting them horribly.
144 They uttered the most awful cries and fell into violent contortions.
145 Jesus turned toward them and commanded them to be silent, to be at peace.
146 They instantly stood still and motionless; then He went up to them, ordered them to be unbound, commanded them to kneel down, prayed, and laid His hands upon them.
147 Under the touch of His hand they sank into a few moments’ unconsciousness, during which the wicked spirits went out of them in the form of a dark vapor.
148 Then their attendants lifted them up, and veiled and in tears, they stood before Jesus, inclining low and giving thanks.
149 He warned them to amend their lives, to purify themselves and do penance, lest their misfortune might come upon them more frightfully than before.
150 It was dusk before Jesus and the disciples, preceded and followed by crowds of people, started at last down the mountain for Gabara.
151 Magdalen, obeying only her impulse without regard to appearances, followed close after Jesus in the crowd of disciples, and her four companions, unwilling to separate from her, did the same.
152 She tried to keep as close to Jesus as she possibly could, though such conduct was quite unusual in females.
153 Some of the disciples called Jesus’ attention to the fact, remarking at the same time what I have just observed.
154 But Jesus, turning around to them, replied: “Let them alone! It is not your affair!” And so He entered the city.
155 When He reached the hall in which Simon Zabulon had prepared the feast, He found the forecourt filled with the sick and the poor who had crowded thither on His approach, and who were loudly calling upon Him for help.
156 Jesus at once turned to them, exhorting, consoling, and healing them.
157 Meanwhile Simon Zabulon, with some other Pharisees, made his appearance.
158 He begged Jesus to come in to the feast, for they were awaiting Him.
159 “Thou hast,” he continued, “already done enough for today.
160 Let these people wait till another time, and let the poor go off at once.
161 But Jesus replied: “These are My guests.
162 I have invited them, and I must first see to their entertainment.
163 When thou didst invite Me to thy feast, thou didst invite them also.
164 I shall not go into thy feast until they are helped, and then even I will go in only with them.
165 Then the Pharisees had to go and prepare tables around the court for the cured and the poor.
166 Jesus cured all, and the disciples led those that wished to remain to the tables prepared for them, and lamps were lighted in the court.
167 Magdalen and the women had followed Jesus hither.
168 They stood in one of the halls of the court adjoining the entertainment hall.
169 Jesus, followed by some of the disciples, went to the table in the latter and from its sumptuous dishes sent various meats to the tables of the poor.
170 The disciples were the bearers of these gifts; they likewise served and ate with the poor.
171 Jesus continued His instructions during the entertainment.
172 The Pharisees were in animated discussion with Him when Magdalen, who with her companions had approached the entrance, all on a sudden darted into the hall.
173 Inclining humbly, her head veiled, in her hand a little white flask closed with a tiny bunch of aromatic herbs instead of a stopper, she glided quickly into the center of the apartment, went behind Jesus, and poured the contents of her little flask over His head.
174 Then catching up the long end of her veil, she folded it, and with both hands passed it lightly once over Jesus’ head, as if wishing to smooth His hair and to arrest the overflow of the ointment.
175 The whole affair occupied but a few instants, and after it Magdalen retired some steps.
176 The discussion carried on so hotly at the moment suddenly ceased.
177 A hush fell upon the company, and they gazed upon Jesus and the woman.
178 The air was redolent with the fragrance of the ointment.
179 Jesus was silent.
180 Some of the guests put their heads together, glanced indignantly at Magdalen, and exchanged whispers.
181 Simon Zabulon especially appeared scandalized.
182 At last Jesus said to him: “Simon, I know well of what thou art thinking! Thou thinkest it improper that I should allow this woman to anoint My head.
183 Thou art thinking that she is a sinner, but thou art wrong.
184 She, out of love, has fulfilled what thou didst leave undone.
185 Thou hast not shown Me the honor due to guests.
186 Then He turned to Magdalen, who was still standing there, and said: “Go in peace! Much has been forgiven thee.
187 At these words Magdalen rejoined her companions, and they left the house together.
188 Then Jesus spoke of her to the guests.
189 He called her a good woman full of compassion.
190 He censured the criticizing of others, public accusations, and remarks upon the exterior fault of others while the speakers often hid in their own hearts much greater, though secret evils.
191 Jesus continued speaking and teaching for a considerable time, and then returned with His followers to the inn.
192 Magdalen was deeply touched and impressed by all she had seen and heard.
193 She was interiorly vanquished.
194 And because she was possessed of a certain impetuous spirit of self-sacrifice, a certain greatness of soul, she longed to do something to honor Jesus and to testify to Him her emotion.
195 She had noticed with chagrin that neither before nor during the meal had He, the most wonderful, the holiest of teachers, He, the most compassionate, the most miraculous Helper of mankind, received from these Pharisees any mark of honor, any of those polite attentions usually extended to guests, and therefore she felt herself impelled to do what she had done.
196 The words of Jesus, “If even one would be moved to come to Me!” still lingered in her memory.
197 The little flask, which was about a hand in height, she generally carried with her as do the grand ladies of our own day.
198 Magdalen’s dress was white, embroidered with large red flowers and tiny green leaves.
199 The sleeves were wide, gathered in and fastened by bracelets.
200 The robe was cut wide and hung loose in the back.
201 It was open in front to just above the knee, where it was caught by straps, or cords.
202 The bodice, both back and front, was ornamented with cords and jewels.
203 It passed over the shoulders like a scapular and was fastened at the sides; under it was another colored tunic.
204 The veil that she usually wound about her neck she had, on entering the banquet hall, opened wide and thrown over her whole person.
205 Magdalen was taller than all the other women, robust, but yet graceful.
206 She had very beautiful, tapering fingers, a small, delicate foot, a wealth of beautiful long hair, and there was something imposing in all her movements.
207 When Magdalen returned to the inn with her companions, Martha took her to another about an hour distant and near the baths of Bethulia.
208 There she found Mary and the holy women awaiting her coming, Mary conversed with her.
209 Magdalen gave an account of Jesus’ discourse, while the two other women related the circumstances of Magdalen’s anointing and Jesus’ words to her.
210 All insisted on Magdalen’s remaining and going back with them, at least for awhile, to Bethania.
211 But she replied that she must return to Magdalum to make some arrangements in her household, a resolution very distasteful to her pious friends.
212 She could not, however, cease talking of the impressions she had received and of the majesty, force, sweetness, and miracles of Jesus.
213 She felt that she must follow Him, that her own life was an unworthy one, and that she ought to join her sister and friends.
214 She became very thoughtful, she wept from time to time, and her heart grew lighter.
215 Nevertheless, she could not be induced to remain, so she returned to Magdalum with her maid.
216 Martha accompanied her a part of the way, and then joined the holy women who were going back to Capharnaum.
217 Magdalen was taller and more beautiful than the other women.
218 Dina, however, was much more active and dexterous, very cheerful, ever ready to oblige, like a lively, affectionate girl, and she was moreover very humble.
219 But the Blessed Virgin surpassed them all in her marvelous beauty.
220 Although in external loveliness she may have had her equal, and may have even been excelled by Magdalen in certain striking features, yet she far outshone them all in her indescribable air of simplicity, modesty, earnestness, sweetness, and gentleness.
221 She was so very pure, so free from all earthly impressions that in her one saw only the reflex image of God in His creature.
222 No one’s bearing resembled hers, except that of her Son.
223 Her countenance surpassed that of all women in its unspeakable purity, innocence, gravity, wisdom, peace, and sweet, devout loveliness.
224 Her whole appearance was noble, and yet she was like a simple, innocent child.
225 She was very grave, very quiet, and often pensive, but never did her sadness destroy the beauty of her countenance, for her tears flowed softly down her placid face.
226 Magdalen was soon again in her old track.
227 She received the visits of men who spoke in the usual disparaging way of Jesus, His journeys, His doctrine, and of all who followed Him.
228 They ridiculed what they heard of Magdalen’s visit to Gabara, and looked upon it as a very unlikely story.
229 As for the rest, they declared that they found Magdalen more beautiful and charming than ever.
230 It was by such speeches that Magdalen allowed herself to be infatuated and her good impressions dissipated.
231 She soon sank deeper than before, and her relapse into sin gave the devil greater power over her.
232 He attacked her more vigorously when he saw that he might possibly lose her.
233 She became possessed, and often fell into cramps and convulsions.
