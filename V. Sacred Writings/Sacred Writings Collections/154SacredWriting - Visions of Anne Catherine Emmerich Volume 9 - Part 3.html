Journey of Jesus to the country of the Magi Kings and Egypt

Chapter 1

Jesus on the Way to Bethania. The Raising of Lazarus

1 As Jesus was tarrying in a little place near Samaria where too the Blessed Virgin and Mary Cleophas were come to spend the Sabbath, they received the news of Lazarus’ death.
2 After this event, which happened in Bethania, his sisters left that place and went to their country house near Ginaea, with the intention of there meeting Jesus and the Blessed Virgin.
3 The remains of Lazarus were embalmed and swathed in linen bands, according to the Jewish custom, and then laid in a coffin of woven rods with a convex cover.
4 All the Apostles were again united around Jesus.
5 They went in several bands to Ginaea, where Jesus taught in the synagogue and, after the closing exercises of the Sabbath, went out to Lazarus’ country house.
6 There they found the Blessed Virgin, who had gone on before.
7 Magdalen came to meet Jesus and to tell Him of her brother’s death, adding the words: “Lord, if Thou hadst been here, my brother had not died!” Jesus replied that his time was not yet come and that it was well that he had died.
8 Still He told the two sisters to allow all the effects of their brother to remain at Bethania, for that He Himself would go there shortly.
9 The holy women, therefore, set out for Bethania, while Jesus and the Apostles returned to Ginaea, from which they went to the inn one hour distant from Bethania.
10 Here another messenger came to Him bearing the earnest request of the sisters that He should repair to Bethania, but He still delayed to go.
11 He rebuked the disciples for their murmuring and impatience at His delaying so long to go to Bethania.
12 He was always like one who could not give an account of His views and actions to them, because they did not understand Him.
13 In His instructions to them He was always more desirous of discovering to them their own thoughts and, on account of their earthly mindedness, of arousing in them distrust of self than of informing them of the reasons of things that they could not comprehend.
14 He still taught upon the laborers in the vineyard, and when the mother of James and John heard Him speak of the near fulfillment of His mission, she thought it only proper that His own relatives should have honorable posts in His Kingdom.
15 She consequently approached Him with a petition to that effect, but He sternly rebuked her.
16 At last Jesus turned His steps to Bethania, continuing all along the way His instructions to the Apostles.
17 Lazarus’ estate stood partly within the walls surrounding the environs of the city, and partly—that is, a portion of the garden and courtyard—outside those walls, which were now going to ruin.
18 Lazarus was eight days dead.
19 They had kept him four days in the hope that Jesus would come and raise him to life.
20 His sisters, as I have said, went to the country house near Ginaea, to meet Jesus; but when they found that He was still resolved not to go back with them, they had returned to Bethania and buried their brother.
21 Their friends, men and women from the city and from Jerusalem, were now gathered around them, lamenting the dead as was the custom.
22 It seems to me that it was toward evening when Mary Zebedeus went in to Martha, who was sitting among the women, and said to her softly that the Lord was coming.
23 Martha arose and went out with her into the garden back of the house.
24 There in an arbor was Magdalen sitting alone.
25 Martha told her that Jesus was near, for through love for Magdalen, she wanted her to be the first to meet the Lord.
26 But I did not see Magdalen go to Jesus, for when He was alone with the Apostles and disciples He did not allow women easy access to Him.
27 It was already growing dusk when Magdalen went back to the women and took Martha’s place, who then went out to meet Jesus.
28 He was standing with the Apostles and some others on the confines of their garden before an open arbor.
29 Martha spoke to Jesus and then turned back to Magdalen, who also by this time had come up.
30 She threw herself at Jesus’ feet, saying: “If Thou hadst been here, he would not have died!” All present were in tears.
31 Jesus too mourned and wept, and delivered a discourse of great length upon death.
32 Many of the audience, which was constantly increasing outside the bower, whispered to one another and murmured their dissatisfaction at Jesus’ not having kept Lazarus alive.
33 It seems to me that it was very early in the morning when Jesus went with the Apostles to the tomb.
34 Mary, Lazarus’ sisters, and others, in all about seven women, were likewise there, as also a crowd of people which was constantly on the increase.
35 Indeed the throng presented somewhat the appearance of a tumult, as upon the day of Christ’s Crucifixion.
36 They proceeded along a road upon either side of which was a thick, green hedge, then passed through a gate, after which about a quarter of an hour’s distance brought them to the walled-in cemetery of Bethania.
37 From the gate of the cemetery, a road led right and left around a hill through which ran a vault.
38 The latter was divided by railings into compartments, and the opening at the end was closed by a grate.
39 One could, from the entrance, see through the whole length of the vault and the green branches of the trees waving outside the opposite end.
40 Light was admitted from openings above.
41 Lazarus’ tomb was the first on the right of the entrance to the vault, down into which some steps led.
42 It was a four-cornered, oblong cave, about three feet in depth, and covered with a flat stone.
43 In it lay the corpse in a lightly woven coffin, and around it in the tomb there was room for one to walk.
44 Jesus with some of the Apostles went down into the vault, while the holy women, Magdalen, and Martha remained standing in the doorway.
45 But the crowd pressed around so that many people climbed up on the roof of the vault and the cemetery walls in order to see.
46 Jesus commanded the Apostles to raise the stone from the grave.
47 They did so, rested it against the wall, and then removed a light cover or door that closed the tomb below that stone.
48 It was at this point of the proceedings that Martha said: “Lord, by this time he stinketh, for he is now of four days.
49 After that they took the lightly woven cover from the coffin, and disclosed the corpse lying in its winding sheet.
50 At that instant Jesus raised His eyes to Heaven, prayed aloud, and called out in a strong voice: “Lazarus, come forth!” At this cry, the corpse arose to a sitting posture.
51 The crowd now pressed with so much violence that Jesus ordered them to be driven outside the walls of the cemetery.
52 The Apostles, who were standing in the tomb by the coffin, removed the handkerchief from Lazarus’ face, unbound his hands and feet, and drew off the winding sheet.
53 Lazarus, as if waking from lethargy, rose from the coffin and stepped out of the grave, tottering and looking like a phantom.
54 The Apostles threw a mantle around him.
55 Like one walking in sleep, he approached the door, passed the Lord and went out to where his sisters and the other women had stepped back in fright as before a ghost.
56 Without daring to touch him, they fell prostrate on the ground.
57 At the same instant, Jesus stepped after him out of the vault and seized him by both hands, His whole manner full of loving earnestness.
58 And now all moved on toward Lazarus’ house.
59 The throng was great.
60 But a certain fear prevailed among the people; consequently the procession formed by Lazarus and his friends was not impeded in its movements by the crowd that followed.
61 Lazarus moved along more like one floating than walking, and he still had all the appearance of a corpse.
62 Jesus walked by his side, and the rest of the party followed sobbing and weeping around them in silent, frightened amazement.
63 They reached the old gate, and went along the road bordered by verdant hedges to the avenue of trees from which they had started.
64 The Lord entered it with Lazarus and His followers, while the crowd thronged outside, clamoring and shouting.
65 At this moment Lazarus threw himself prostrate on the earth before Jesus, like one about to be received into a Religious Order.
66 Jesus spoke some words, and then they went on to the house, about a hundred paces distant.
67 Jesus, the Apostles, and Lazarus were alone in the dining hall.
68 The Apostles formed a circle around Jesus and Lazarus, who was kneeling before the Lord.
69 Jesus laid His right hand on his head and breathed upon him seven times.
70 The Lord’s breath was luminous.
71 I saw a dark vapor withdrawing as it were from Lazarus, and the devil under the form of a black winged figure, impotent and wrathful, clearing the circle backward and mounting on high.
72 By this ceremony, Jesus consecrated Lazarus to His service, purified him from all connection with the world and sin, and strengthened him with the gifts of the Holy Ghost.
73 He made him a long address in which He told him that He had raised him to life that he might serve Him, and that he would have to endure great persecution on the part of the Jews.
74 Up to this time, Lazarus was in his grave clothes, but now he retired to lay them aside and put on his own garments.
75 It was at this moment that his sisters and friends embraced him for the first time, for before this there was something so corpselike about him that it inspired terror.
76 I saw meanwhile that Lazarus’ soul, during the time of its separation from his body, was in a place peaceful and painless, lighted by only a glimmering twilight, and that while there he related to the just, Joseph, Joachim, Anne, Zachary, John, etc.,how things were going with the Redeemer on earth.
77 By the Saviour’s breathing upon him, Lazarus received the seven gifts of the Holy Ghost and was perfectly freed from connection with earthly things.
78 He received those gifts before the Apostles, for he had by his death become acquainted with great mysteries, had gazed upon another world.
79 He had actually been dead, and he was now born again.
80 He could therefore receive those gifts.
81 Lazarus comprises in himself a deep significance and a profound mystery.
82 And now a meal was ready, and all reclined at table upon which were many dishes and little jugs.
83 A man served.
84 After the meal the women entered, but remained at the lower end of the hall, to hear the teachings of Jesus.
85 Lazarus was sitting next Him.
86 There was a frightful noise around the house, for many had come out from Jerusalem, even the guards, and were now besetting the house.
87 But Jesus sent the Apostles out, to drive off both people and guards.
88 Jesus continued His instruction till after lamplight, and told the disciples that He was going next morning with two Apostles to Jerusalem.
89 When they placed before Him the danger attending such a step, He replied that He would not be recognized, that He would not go openly.
90 I saw them afterward taking a little sleep, leaning around against the wall.
91 Before daybreak Jesus, accompanied by John and Matthew, who had girded up their garments somewhat differently from their usual custom, started from Bethania for Jerusalem.
92 They went around the city and, taking byroads, reached the house in which later on the Last Supper was celebrated.
93 There they remained quietly the whole day and the next night, Jesus instructing and confirming His friends of the city.
94 I saw Mary Marcus and Veronica in the house, and fully a dozen men.
95 Nicodemus, to whom the house belonged, but who had gladly resigned it for the use of Jesus’ friends, was not there.
96 He had on that very day gone to Bethania to see Lazarus.
97 I saw also a gathering of Pharisees and High Priests who had come together to discuss Jesus and Lazarus.
98 Among other things I heard them say that they feared Jesus would raise all the dead, and then what confusion would ensue!
99 At noon on that day, a great tumult arose in Bethania.
100 If Jesus had been there, they would have stoned Him.
101 Lazarus was obliged to hide, and the Apostles, to slip away in different directions.
102 All the other friends of Jesus in Bethania were likewise forced to lie in concealment.
103 Minds became calm, however, when people took into consideration that they had no right to take action against Lazarus.
104 Jesus passed the whole night till early next morning in the house on Mount Sion.
105 Before day He left Jerusalem with Matthew and John and fled across the Jordan, not by the route He had formerly taken on the side of Bethabara, but by another off to the northeast.
106 It may have been toward noon when He reached the opposite shore of the Jordan.
107 That evening the Apostles from Bethania joined Him, and they spent the night under a great tree.
108 In the morning they started for a little village in the neighborhood, and on their way found a blind man lying on the roadside.
109 He was in charge of two boys, who were not, however, related to him.
110 He was a shepherd from the region of Jericho.
111 He had heard from the Apostles that the Lord was coming that way, and he was now crying out to Him for a cure.
112 Jesus laid His hand on his head, and the man received his sight.
113 Then he cast off his old rags and, in his undergarment, followed Jesus to the village, where in a hall Jesus taught of following Him.
114 He said that they who wanted to do so must, as the blind man did his rags, leave all, to follow Him with full use of their sight.
115 A mantle was given to the man cured of blindness.
116 He wanted to join Jesus at once, but he was put off till he should prove his constancy.
117 Jesus taught here until nearly evening.
118 There were about eight Apostles with Him.
119 After that, as He drew near a little city, Jesus was hungry.
120 I could not help smiling at the thought of His being hungry, for Jesus’ hunger was very different from that of others.
121 He was hungering after souls.
122 From the last place that He had visited, some people who had not the right dispositions went with Him.
123 On the roadside stood a fig tree that bore no fruit.
124 Jesus went up to the tree and cursed it.
125 It withered on the instant, its leaves turning yellow, and the trunk becoming crooked.
126 Jesus taught in the school upon the sterile fig tree.
127 There were some malevolent Doctors and Pharisees who invited Jesus to take His departure.
128 A little stream spanned by a bridge ran by this place1 into the Jordan.
129 The school was built on an eminence.
130 Jesus and His party spent the night at an inn.


Chapter 2

Jesus Begins the Journey Into the Land of the Three Holy Kings

1 Next day when Jesus and His companions left that last place, they took a northeasterly direction through the land of the tribe of Gad.
2 I heard Jesus saying whither He was now about to journey.
3 He told the Apostles and disciples that they should separate from Him, designated to them where they should and where they should not teach, and where they should again join Him.
4 He was now, He said, about to make an extraordinary journey.
5 He would spend the next Sabbath in Great Corozain, then go to Bethsaida, and from there to the south into the region of Machaerus and Madian.
6 Thence He would proceed to where Agar had exposed Ismael,1 and Jacob had set up the ston.
7 Then He would journey to the east around the Dead Sea and on to the place upon which Melchisedech had offered sacrifice before Abraham.
8 On this site there stands today a chapel, in which Divine Service is sometimes celebrated.
9 It is built of red stone, and overgrown with moss.
10 Jesus declared His intention of going likewise to Heliopolis in Egypt, where He had once dwelt in childhood.
11 There were some good people there who as children had played with Him, and who had not entirely forgotten Him.
12 They were constantly asking what had become of Him, but they could not believe that He of whom they heard so much was the Child of their remembrance.
13 He will return from the other side through Hebron and the valley of Josaphat, pass the place at which He had been baptized by John, and through the desert in which He had been tempted.
14 He announced that His absence would be for about three months, and that His followers would be sure to find Him at the end of that time at Jacob’s Well near Sichar, though they might meet Him before that, when He would be returning through Judea.
15 He gave them minute instructions in a long discourse, above all as to how they should during His absence conduct themselves in their missionary duties.
16 I remember these words, that wherever they were not well received, they should shake the dust from their shoes.
17 Matthew returned home for awhile.
18 He was a married man.
19 His wife was a very virtuous person and, since Matthew’s vocation, they had lived in perfect continency.
20 He was to teach in his own home, and quietly put up with the contempt of his former associates.
21 In Great Corozain, Jesus taught on the Sabbath in the synagogue.
22 Peter, Andrew, and Philip were with Him.
23 Toward noon a man from Capharnaum, who had been waiting for Jesus, approached Him, His son, he said, was sick unto death, and He implored the Lord to go with him and cure him.
24 But Jesus commanded him to return home, for his son was already restored to health.
25 There were many others gathered around Jesus, some belonging to the city, and others from a distance.
26 Some were sick and looking for a cure, others were in search of consolation.
27 He satisfied some at once, but to others He held out the promise of future assistance.
28 On the evening of that Sabbath, Jesus took leave of the inhabitants outside the synagogue, and proceeded with several of the Apostles up to where the Jordan empties into the sea, in order to cross to the other side.
29 The ferry was higher up, and that made the journey much longer.
30 Here they crossed on a kind of raft formed of beams laid one over another like a grating.
31 In the center, on a raised platform, was a coop, or little half-tub into which the water could not penetrate, and there the baggage of the passengers was deposited.
32 The raft was propelled by means of long poles.
33 The shore of the Jordan was not very high in this place, and it seems to me there were some little islands lying around in this part of the river.
34 I saw the Lord and the three Apostles travelling by moonlight.
35 Outside of Bethsaida, as was customary at the entrance to the cities of Palestine, stood a long shed under which travelers used to ungird their garments and brush off the dust of travel before entering the city; generally some people were to be found there to wash their feet.
36 This was the case on the arrival of the Lord and the Apostles, after which they repaired to Andrew’s, where they partook of a meal of honey, rolls, and grapes.
37 Andrew was married, and his house was by no means a small one.
38 It had a courtyard, was surrounded by walls, and was situated at one side of the city.
39 Peter and Philip accompanied the Lord, but Andrew went on ahead.
40 There were in all twelve men present at the meal, and at the end of it, six women came in to hear Jesus’ teaching.
41 Next day, as He was leaving Bethsaida with the three Apostles, He paused for awhile in a house outside the city in which were all kinds of goods and chattels peculiar to fishing.
42 A great many men were assembled there, and Jesus gave them an instruction.
43 Setting out at last, He journeyed up the shore of the Jordan, crossed the bridge far above the ferry just mentioned, and proceeded through eastern Galilee to the land of Basan.
44 I saw in a region beyond the Jordan, a district covered with white sand and tiny white pebbles, several disciples in an open shepherd shed awaiting the Lord’s coming.
45 They had brought with them three youths, tall and slim.
46 While awaiting Jesus, the disciples had gathered yellow and green berries as large as figs, also little yellow apples that grew some on bushes, others on trees, from which they broke them off with chopping sticks.
47 The road by which Jesus and the three Apostles came appeared to be not much frequented, for it was overgrown with long grass, and extended under an avenue of spreading fruit trees whose branches interlaced overhead.
48 The Apostles broke off some of the fruit and put it into their pockets, but Jesus took none.
49 He had travelled all night through mountainous districts.
50 The disciples who had been awaiting His coming now went forward to meet Him.
51 They pressed around Him with words of salutation, but without offering their hands.
52 In front of the shed lay a long, broad, four-cornered log, around which Jesus and the others threw themselves in a reclining posture as at table, and before each was placed a portion of the fruit just gathered.
53 They had brought with them also little jugs containing some kind of beverage.
54 Off in the distance lay a city and behind it rose a mountain chain.
55 I think this region was in the land of the Amorrhites.
56 From this place the road again took a downward direction.
57 I saw Jesus and His companions journeying the whole day and, in the evening, arriving at a little scattered village.
58 On the roadside stood an inn.
59 The travelers entered and were soon surrounded by a crowd of inquisitive people.
60 They had not heard much of Jesus, but they were for the most part good and simple hearted.
61 Jesus related to them the parable of the good shepherd, and then travelled on a short distance to another inn, at which He and His followers ate and slept.
62 The Lord told the latter that He intended to go alone with the three youths through Chaldea and the land of Ur, Abraham’s birthplace, and thence through Arabia to Egypt.
63 The disciples should scatter here throughout the district and instruct the inhabitants; as for Himself, He added, He would teach wherever He went.
64 In fine He again told them that, at the end of three months, they would meet at the Well of Jacob near Sichar.
65 I saw Simeon, Cleophas, and Saturnin among the disciples.
66 At dawn of day Jesus bade farewell to the Apostles and disciples, to each of whom He extended His hand.
67 They were very much troubled at His taking with Him only the three youths.
68 These youths were from sixteen to eighteen years old and very different from the Jews.
69 They were more slender and active, and wore long garments.
70 They were like children to Jesus, whom they waited on most affectionately.
71 Whenever they came to water, they washed His feet.
72 They ran off on the road here and there, and came back with little rods, flowers, fruits, and berries.
73 Jesus instructed them most lovingly and explained to them in parables all that had happened up to that time.
74 The parents of these youths belonged to the family of Mensor.
75 They had come to Palestine with the caravan of the Three Kings and, at the departure of the same for home, had remained behind among the shepherds in the Valley of the Shepherds.
76 They became Jews, married the daughters of the shepherds, and came into possession of meadow lands between Samaria and Jericho.
77 The youngest of the youths was named Eremenzear and later on was called Hermas.
78 He was the boy whom Jesus, at the prayer of his mother, had cured in the region of Sichar, after His interview with the Samaritan at Jacob’s Well.
79 The next one was Sela, or Silas; and the eldest, Eliud, received in Baptism the name of Siricius.
80 They were called, also, the secret disciples, and at a later period they were associated with Thomas, John, and Paul.
81 Eremenzear wrote an account of this journey.
82 On this journey, Jesus wore a brownish tunic, knitted or woven, that fell around Him in folds long and full; over that He had a long garment of fine white wool with wide sleeves.
83 It was fastened at the waist by a broad girdle of the same material as the scarf that He wound around His head when sleeping.
84 Jesus was taller than the Apostles.
85 Walking or standing, His fair, grave face rose above them.
86 His step was firm, His bearing erect.
87 He was neither thin nor stout, but nobly formed with an appearance of perfect health.
88 His shoulders were broad, and His chest well developed.
89 Exercise and travelling had strengthened His muscles, although they presented no sign of hard labor.
90 The road taken by Jesus and the youths after parting from the Apostles was a constantly ascending one in a direction toward the East, over a white, sandy soil and through cedars and date trees.
91 Opposite arose the mountains of Galaad.
92 Jesus wanted to spend the coming Sabbath in the last Jewish city met in this direction.
93 I think it was called Cedar.
94 Jesus and the youths ate on the way the fruits of the trees and berries.
95 The youths carried pouches filled with little rolls, jugs containing some kind of drink, and staves.
96 The Lord sometimes broke off a staff for Himself from a tree in passing, and again cast it aside.
97 His feet, otherwise bare, were protected by sandals.
98 In the evening they went to some solitary house occupied by rude, simple people, and there slept for the night.
99 Jesus nowhere made Himself known, although He everywhere taught in beautiful parables of all kinds, but principally in those relating to the good shepherd.
100 The people questioned Him about Jesus of Nazareth, but He did not tell that it was Himself.
101 He in turn put questions to them concerning their work, their business affairs, so that they concluded He was a travelling shepherd looking around after good pasture lands, as was often the case in Jewish countries.
102 I did not see Him effect any cure nor work any miracle in these parts.
103 Next morning He journeyed on.
104 He may now have still been some miles from Cedar, which was built on rising ground, the mountain chain behind it.
105 Abraham’s fatherland was in this direction, but far off toward the northeast; the land of the Three Kings was toward the southeast.
106 Some of the disciples had returned to their homes, while others had scattered around the country teaching.
107 Zacheus of Jericho accompanied them awhile, after which he returned home, gave up his business, sold all that he had, bestowed the proceeds upon the poor, and went with his wife (with whom he henceforth lived in continency) to another place.
108 The Lord told the disciples that nine weeks would pass before they should join Him again.
109 The excitement in Jerusalem on account of Lazarus was very great.
110 Jesus absented Himself during it, that people might lose sight of Him, while the conviction of the truth of this miracle disposed many to conversion.
111 When Jesus returned He was very thin.
112 There is no written account of this journey, since no Apostle accompanied the Lord on it; perhaps too the Apostles did not even know of all the places in which He had been.
113 As well as I remember, I then saw this road for the first time.
114 Jesus journeyed on with His three young companions to the southeast, taking byways most frequently, and spending the night, like the preceding one among the shepherds, in a solitary house.
115 The people of these parts were good and artless.
116 They gazed at Jesus in wonder, and loved Him at once.
117 He related to them many of the parables He was accustomed to use in Judea, and to them they listened with delight.
118 But He neither healed nor blessed.
119 When they asked Him about Jesus of Nazareth, He answered by telling them about those that had quitted all to follow Him, and then passed to parables that explained what He had said.
120 The people thought He was a shepherd looking around for herds or meadows.


Chapter 3

Jesus in Cedar

1 Jesus and the youths reached Cedar before the Sabbath.
2 They had not travelled by the highroad, but by roundabout ways.
3 As it was too late to enter the city, they passed the night at a large public inn at which other wayfarers had sought shelter.
4 There were open sheds with sleeping accommodations in the enclosure, and the whole was surrounded by a courtyard.
5 A man, the one that superintended the establishment, unlocked the inn, after which he returned to the city.
6 Next morning, he came out again to the inn, and then received a small sum for his services.
7 The travelers went their several ways, but the superintendent took Jesus and His companions back with him to his own house in the city.
8 Cedar was situated at the foot of a mountain, in a valley through which flowed a river.
9 It consisted of an old and a new city separated by the little river which flowed from the east and off toward Palestine.
10 The shore was very steep, and the river was spanned by two arches very solidly built.
11 On this side the place was poor and insignificant, and inhabited principally by Jewish shepherds who likewise engaged in the manufacture of light huts, shepherd and stable utensils.
12 On the opposite side, Cedar presented a more opulent appearance.
13 There were no Jews there, but only heathens.
14 The Jewish costume was somewhat modified here, for some of the people wore a pointed cap.
15 In the city this side of the river, there was a synagogue, and upon a square surrounded by grass plots and walks of clean white sand, played a fountain.
16 This was the most beautiful spot in the city.
17 The Lord and the boys went with their host to the synagogue, and quietly celebrated the Sabbath.
18 At the end of the prayers, Jesus asked whether He might venture to relate something to them, and when the good people showed their willingness to listen, He recounted the parable of the Prodigal Son.
19 They listened attentively, admired Him greatly, but knew not who He was.
20 He called Himself a shepherd seeking the lost lambs in order to lead them into good pasture.
21 They regarded Him as a Prophet and, during the rest of the day, conducted Him to their houses where too He taught.
22 The next day He gave an instruction at the fountain.
23 The men and women sat at His feet, and He pressed the children to His breast.
24 He told them about Zacheus climbing up the fig tree, of his leaving all and following Him; of him who in the Temple had said: “I thank God that I am not like the publican”; and lastly, of that other who, striking his breast, said: “Lord, be merciful to me, a poor sinner!” The inhabitants of Cedar became very fond of Jesus and thought no harm of Him.
25 They begged Him to stay with them till the next Sabbath and then teach again in their school, and when they asked Him about Jesus of Nazareth, He related to them many things of Him and His doctrine.
26 On leaving this place, Jesus and His travelling companions proceeded eastward from Cedar into a country of beautiful meadowlands and palm trees, and thence to Edon.
27 On the way, He visited a house that stood off by itself, and in which both the father and mother of the family had long been bedridden with incurable maladies.
28 Several children were going and coming around the house.
29 All were good.
30 Here also they asked Him about Jesus of Nazareth, of whom they had heard divers reports.
31 Jesus answered them in a beautiful parable of a king and his son, in which he spoke of the One of whom they inquired.
32 He told them that He would be persecuted, and that He would return to His Father’s Kingdom, which He would share with all those that had followed Him.
33 As Jesus spoke I had a vision of His Passion, His Ascension, His throne surrounded by all the angels and set next His Father’s, meaning His dominion over the world; and, lastly, I saw the reward portioned out to His followers.
34 I saw likewise the vision of His Kingdom and the whole parable that He was relating to the people, and I saw too that He impressed upon their hearts a lasting picture of it.
35 When He asked them whether they believed all He had told them and whether they would follow the good King, and they had protested their belief and their willingness, He promised the two old people that God would reward them by curing them and allowing them to follow Him to Edon.
36 And all on a sudden, they were restored to health and, to the astonishment of the beholders, were indeed able to follow Jesus to Edon.
37 The man’s name was Benjamin, and he was a direct descendant from Ruth.
38 I think that Titus was either a son or a relative of this couple so suddenly cured.
39 He was at that time between fourteen to sixteen years old.
40 He went to Cedar and to every other place in this region in which Jesus taught, in order to hear Him and to listen to others talking about Him.
41 Marcus, whose birthplace was nearer Judea, was acquainted with this family, and so too was Silas.
42 Jesus and the three youths, on leaving that house, went on to Edon through lovely fields and meadows shaded by palm trees.
43 Jesus carried a shepherd’s crook in His right hand.
44 In the public feast house, on a large, open square to the left of the entrance to the city, a marriage was being celebrated.
45 The house contained a large hall, at the end of which was the kitchen.
46 All around it were sleeping apartments, in each of which there were three beds that could be separated from one another by an ornamented screen.
47 Although it was clear daylight, a lamp was burning in the hall.
48 The guests, male and female, as also the bride and bridegroom, adorned with flowery wreaths, were all assembled in the same apartment.
49 Boys were singing and playing upon flutes and other instruments.
50 These pious people were awaiting Jesus, whom they looked upon as a Prophet.

5 To Salathiel He said: “Thou hast allowed thy heart to be moved by the beauty of thy wife! But think how great the beauty of the soul must be, since God sends His Son upon earth to save souls by the sacrifice of His Body! Whoever serves the body, serves not the soul.
6 Beauty inflames concupiscence, and concupiscence corrupts the soul.
7 Incontinence is like a creeping plant that chokes and destroys the wheat and the vines.
8 These last words turned the instruction again upon the subject of vine and wheat culture, and Jesus warned His hearers to keep far from their fields and vineyards two running weeds which He designated by name.
9 At last He announced to them that on the coming Sabbath He would teach in the school at Cedar, and on that occasion they would hear what they must do to become His followers and share in His Kingdom.
0 He told them, moreover, that He would then depart from that region and journey eastward to Arabia.
1 When they asked Him why He was going among those heathens, those star-worshippers, He answered that He had friends among them who had followed a star in order to greet Him at His birth.
2 These He wanted to search after, that He might invite them also into the vineyard and the Kingdom of His Father, and put them on the straight road to it.
3 An extraordinarily great multitude assembled in Cedar to meet Jesus, who now began publicly to heal crowds of sick.
4 Sometimes while passing among those that had been brought hither by their friends, He merely pronounced the words: “Arise! Follow Me!”—and they rose up cured.
5 The wonder and admiration produced by these miracles reached such a pitch of enthusiasm that had not Jesus Himself suppressed it, the whole country would have risen in one sudden transport of joy.
6 Salathiel and his wife were among the assembly at Cedar.
7 Jesus once more spoke to them of the duties of the married state, and gave them detailed instructions upon the way in which they should live together in order to become a good vine (that is, one that would produce pure and excellent fruit, such as would become disciples of His Apostles, saints, and martyrs).
8 He inculcated the observance of modesty and purity, bade them in all their actions aim at purity of intention, exhorted them to prayer and renunciation, and rigorously commanded perfect continence after the period of conception.
9 He spoke of the mutual confidence that ought to exist between husband and wife, and of the obedience of the latter to the former.
0 The husband should not keep silence when the wife asks him questions.
1 He ought to respect her and be indulgent toward her, since she is the weaker vessel.
2 He should not mistrust her if he sees her talking with others, neither should she be jealous upon beholding him doing the same; still each should be careful not to give to the other cause for vexation.
3 They should suffer no third party to come in between them, and should settle their little differences themselves.
4 He told the wife that she should become a pious Abigail, and pointed out to them a region suitable for the cultivation of wheat.
5 They must, He said, raise a hedge around their vineyard, which hedge was to consist of the admonitions He had just given them.
6 Before leaving Cedar, Jesus gave in the synagogue another very long instruction, in which He again explained the connection existing between all the points upon which up to that time he had here taught separately.
7 He spoke in simple, childlike allegories of the mysteries of Original Sin, the vicious propagation of the human race, their ever-increasing corruption, the dispositions of God’s grace and His guidance of the chosen people from generation to generation down to the Blessed Virgin, the mystery of the Incarnation and the regeneration of fallen man from death to eternal life through the Son of the Virgin.
8 Here He introduced the parable of the grain of wheat which had to be buried in the ground before it could spring forth into new fruit, but He was not understood by His hearers.
9 He told them that they should follow Him not for a short time only, but on a long journey that would end only at the Judgment.

1 Judgment comes like a thief in the night; death strikes at every hour.
2 They, the Ishmaelite, were typified by the servants, and they ought to be faithful.
3 Melchisedech, He said, was a type of Himself.
4 His sacrifice consisted of bread and wine, but in Him they would be changed into flesh and blood.
5 At last Jesus told them in plain terms that He was the Redeemer.
6 At this revelation, many became timid and fearful, while others grew more ardent and enthusiastic in their adherence to Him.
7 He enforced upon them in particular love for one another, compassion, sympathy in joy and sorrow such as the members of the body feel for one another.
8 The pagans from the pagan quarter of Cedar were present at this instruction, to which they listened from a distance.
9 They had been very hostile toward the Jews, but from this time many approached them and questioned them in a friendly manner about Jesus’ doctrine and miracles.
