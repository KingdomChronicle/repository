The First Pasch of Jerusalem

Chapter 1

Jesus in Silo, Kibzaim, and Thebez

1 I next saw Jesus in Silo, a city built around a high, steep rock with an extended plateau on a gently rising mountain range.
2 On this plateau, the highest elevation of the mountain range, in early times after the departure from Egypt and during the journey through the desert, the Tabernacle with the Ark of the Covenant had rested.
3 There was a large space surrounded by a wall partly in ruins, and in it might still be seen the remains of the little building that had been erected over the Tabernacle.
4 On the spot whereon the Ark had stood, under a roof which rested upon open arches, was a pillar similar to the one in Gilgal, and under it a kind of vault excavated in the rocky foundation.
5 Not far from the spot occupied by the Ark was a place for offering sacrifice and a covered pit for the reception of the refuse of the slaughter, for they were permitted to offer sacrifice here three or four times in the year.
6 The synagogue also was built on this enclosed space of the plateau, from which was presented a widely extended view.
7 From it one could see the plateau of Jerusalem, the Sea of Galilee, and far over many mountains.
8 Silo itself was a somewhat dilapidated and not very populous city.
9 It possessed two schools, one belonging to the Pharisees, the other to the Sadducees.
10 But the people were not good; they were arrogant, full of self-conceit and false assurance.
11 At some distance from the city gate with its dilapidated towers, stood an Essenian cloister now fallen to ruin, and nearer to the city was the house wherein the Benjaminites had confined the virgins whom, at the Feast of Tabernacles, they had brought captive to Silo.( 21:19-24).
12 Jesus with His twelve companions put up at a house at which travelling teachers and prophets were privileged.
13 It was adjoining the schools and dwellings of the Pharisees and Scribes, who had a kind of seminary here.
14 About twenty of these Scribes in their long robes and girdles, with long, rough tufts hanging from their sleeves, gathered around Jesus.
15 They feigned not to know Him, and spoke of Jesus as of a third person using all kinds of cutting speeches, such as: “Now, how will it be? There are two baptisms, that of John and that of Jesus, the carpenter’s son of Galilee.
16 Which, now, will be the right baptism?” They went on to say that they had heard also that women attached themselves to the mother of this carpenter’s son; for instance, a widow with her two sons.
17 These latter, at the instigation of their mother, joined the followers of Jesus, while she herself went with His Mother, and so they travelled about.
18 But as for themselves, they needed not such novelties.
19 They had the Promise and the Law.
20 All this they did not express bluntly and rudely, but with a semblance of mock friendship for Jesus.
21 He answered their pointed speeches by saying that He was the One of whom they were speaking.
22 And when they referred to the voice heard at His baptism, He informed them that it was the voice of His Heavenly Father, who was the Father of everyone who would repent of his sins and be regenerated by Baptism.
23 Then, affecting to consider it a very sacred place, they expressed unwillingness to allow Jesus and His disciples to enter the enclosure where formerly the Ark of the Covenant had stood.
24 But Jesus, heedless of their opposition, entered.
25 He reproached them with having, on account of their wickedness, lost the Ark of the Covenant; that now, preserving only the remembrance of it, they were still just as bad; that they had always violated the Law in the past, as well as in the present; and that, as the Ark had been withdrawn from the keeping of their ancestors, so now would the fulfillment of the Law be taken from themselves.
26 As these men showed a desire to dispute with Him on some points of the Law, He stood them out, two by two, and interrogated them like children, proposing to them many deep questions in the Law.
27 They were unable to answer; so, confused and angry, muttering and nudging one another with the elbow, they began to slink away.
28 Then Jesus led them to the covered pit in which had been thrown the refuse of the sacrifice.
29 He ordered them to uncover it and told them in a similitude that they were like unto that pit, inwardly full of ordure and rottenness and unfit for sacrifice, though outwardly clean, their unsightliness covered over by a fine exterior.
30 He reminded them that from this very spot, as punishment of the sins of their forefathers, the Holy Ark had been taken away.
31 They all left the place in anger.
32 When Jesus taught in the synagogue, He insisted especially upon the reverence due the aged and love toward parents.
33 He spoke warmly on these points, for the people of Silo had long been in the wicked habit of slighting, despising, and disowning their aged parents.
34 A road led to Silo from Bethel on the south.
35 Lebona was not far distant, and to Samaria from Bethel, it may have been from eight to nine hours.
36 The Prophet Jonas lies buried at Silo.
37 When Jesus left Silo from the opposite side of the city, the northwest, Andrew, Saturnin, and Joseph of Arimathea’s nephews separated from Him, and proceeded on ahead to Galilee.
38 Jesus with some disciples of John, then in His company, directed His steps to Kibzaim, where He arrived before the Sabbath.
39 Kibzaim lay in a valley between two branches of a mountain range that extended through the middle of the country, and assumed in this place almost the exact shape of a wolf’s claw.
40 The people were good, hospitable souls, and well-inclined to Jesus, whose coming they were expecting.
41 Kibzaim was a Levitical city.
42 Jesus put up near the school with one of the head men.
43 There arrived also to salute Jesus, Lazarus, Martha, Johanna Chusa, the son of Simeon (who was employed at the Temple), and the old servant of the first named.
44 They were on their way to the wedding at Cana, and had been informed by messengers that they would here meet Jesus.
45 Jesus, from the very first, always treated Lazarus with distinction and as a very dear friend.
46 And yet I never heard Him ask: How is such or such a one of thy relatives or acquaintances?
47 Kibzaim was a solitary place hidden away in a corner of the mountain.
48 The inhabitants subsisted chiefly by the cultivation of fruits.
49 The manufacture of tents and carpets was also carried on, and many were engaged in sandal-making.
50 Jesus spent the Sabbath here, and cured several sick persons by a word of command.
51 Some were dropsical and others simpletons.
52 They were brought on litters to Jesus and set down in front of the school.
53 Jesus took a repast at the house of a distinguished Levite.
54 After the Sabbath He went again to Sichar, where He arrived late, and passed the night at an inn appointed for Him.
55 Lazarus and his party went from Kibzaim straight to Galilee.
56 Early next morning, Jesus went from Sichar northeastwardly toward Thebez.
57 In Sichar, or Sichem, He could not teach.
58 There were no Jews there.
59 The inhabitants were made up of Samaritans and some others who had settled there either after the Babylonian Captivity, or in consequence of a war.
60 They used to go up to the Temple at Jerusalem, though they did not join in the Jewish sacrifices.
61 Near Sichem is that beautiful field which Jacob bought for his son Joseph.
62 A part of it already belonged to Herod of Galilee.
63 A boundary consisting of stakes, a rampart of earth, and a path ran through the valley.
64 Thebez was quite an important city, traversed by a highway and possessed of considerable trade.
65 Heavily laden camels, their burdens rising high upon their backs, came and went.
66 It was something wonderful to see those animals with their packs like so many little towers, climbing slowly over the mountain, their head at the end of the long neck moving from side to side before their lofty burden.
67 Raw silk formed a chief staple of trade.
68 The people of Thebez were not bad, nor were they prejudiced against Jesus, but they were neither simple nor childlike.
69 They were indifferent, as well-to-do trades people often are.
70 The priests and Scribes were content with themselves and indifferent to others.
71 As Jesus entered the city, the possessed and the lunatics raised their cry: “There comes the Prophet of Galilee! He has power over us! He will drive us away!” Jesus commanded them silence, and instantly they became quiet.
72 Jesus put up near the synagogue whither the crowds followed Him, bringing with them their sick, of whom He healed many.
73 That evening He taught in the school and celebrated the Feast of Dedication, which then began.
74 In the school and in all the houses seven lights were lit, also outdoors in the fields and on the roads near the shepherds’ huts were little burning tufts of something on the ends of stakes.
75 Thebez was admirably situated on the mountain.
76 At some distance, one could see the mountain road running through it and the laden camels climbing up; but near the city the view was hidden.
77 Andrew, Saturnin, and Joseph’s nephews had already left Silo and gone to Galilee.
78 Andrew had been up among his relatives at Bethsaida.
79 He had informed Peter that he had again found the Messiah, who was taken on His way up to Galilee, and that he would take him (Peter) to Him.
80 All went now to Arbela, called also Betharbel, to see Nathanael Chased, who was there on business, and to induce him to go with them to celebrate the feast at Gennabris.
81 Chased resided at that time in Gennabris in a high house that, with several others, stood by itself outside the city.
82 The disciples spoke much to him of Jesus.
83 Andrew had purposely taken them there for the feast because he, as well as they, counted upon Nathanael.
84 They were eager to hear his opinion, but Nathanael appeared rather indifferent to the whole affair.
85 Lazarus had brought Martha and Johanna Chusa to Mary then at Capharnaum, whither she had come from Cana.
86 They set off again for Tiberias where they hoped to meet Jesus.
87 Simeon’s son was one of the escorts, and the bridegroom of Cana went also to meet the Lord.
88 This bridegroom was the son of the daughter of Sobe, the sister of Anne.
89 His name was Nathanael.
90 He did not belong to Cana, though he was married there.
91 Gennabris was a populous city.
92 A highway ran through it, and there was much business and traffic carried on, especially in silk.
93 It was in the country, a couple of hours from Tiberias, from which it was separated by mountains.
94 To reach it, one had to go somewhat southward between Emmaus and Tiberias, and then turn to the latter.
95 Arbela was between Sephoris and Tiberias.


Chapter 2

First Formal Call of Peter, Philip, and Nathanael

1 Jesus departed before daybreak from Thebez
. 2 He and His disciples proceeded at first eastward, and then turning to the north, journeyed along the base of the mountain and through the valley of the Jordan toward Tiberias
. 3 He passed through Abelmahula, a beautiful city, where the mountain extends more to the north
. 4 It was the birthplace of Eliseus
. 5 The city is built on a spur of the mountain, and I noticed the great difference between the fruitfulness of its sunny side and its northern one
. 6 The inhabitants were tolerably good
. 7 They had heard of the miracles wrought by Jesus at Kibzaim and Thebez, so they stayed with Him on the way, begging Him to tarry with them and heal their sick
. 8 The excitement became almost tumultuous, but Jesus did not stay with them long
. 9 This city was about four hours from Thebez
. 10 Jesus passed near Scythopolis and on to the Jordan
. 11 As He was journeying from Abelmahula, He met near a little city about six hours from Tiberias, Andrew, Peter, and John
. 12 Leaving the other friends in Gennabris, these three had come on to meet Jesus
. 13 Peter and John were in this part of the country upon some business connected with their fishery
. 14 They intended to proceed direct to Gennabris, but Andrew persuaded them to go first to meet the Lord
. 15 Andrew presented his brother to Jesus, who among other words said to him: This was said at the first salutation
. 16 To John, Jesus addressed some words relative to their next meeting
. 17 Then Peter and John went out to Gennabris, while Andrew accompanied Jesus into the environs of Tarichaea
. 18 John the Baptist had by this time abandoned his place of baptism on this side of the Jordan
. 19 He had crossed the river and was now baptizing about one hour to the north of Bethabara, at the place whereon Jesus had lately allowed the disciples to baptize and where John himself had baptized at an earlier period
. 20 John had made this change to suit the convenience of the people from the region under Philip the Tetrarch
. 21 Philip was a good-natured man
. 22 Many of his people desired baptism, but were unwilling to cross the Jordan to receive it
. 23 Among them were many of the heathens
. 24 The last visit that Jesus made to this part of the country had roused in numbers the desire after baptism
. 25 Another reason also influenced John to baptize where Jesus’ disciples had lately been similarly engaged, and that was to show that there was no disunion between him and Jesus
. 26 When Jesus with Andrew reached the neighborhood of Tarichaea, He put up near the lake at a house belonging to Peter’s fishery
. 27 Andrew had previously given orders for preparations to be made for Jesus’ reception
. 28 Jesus did not go into the city
. 29 There was something dark and repulsive about the inhabitants, who were deeply engaged in usury and thought only of gain
. 30 Simon, who here had some employment, had with Thaddeus and James the Less, his brothers, gone for the feast to Gennabris, where James the Greater and John were
. 31 Lazarus, Saturnin, and Simeon’s son came here to meet Jesus, as also the bridegroom of Cana
. 32 The last named invited Jesus and all His company to his marriage
. 33 The principal motive that led Jesus to pass a couple of days in the vicinity of Tarichaea was that He desired to give the future Apostles and disciples time to communicate to one another the reports circulated about Himself, and especially what Andrew and Saturnin had to relate
. 34 He desired also that, by more frequent intercourse, they should better understand one another
. 35 While Jesus traversed the country around Tarichaea, I saw Andrew remaining in the house
. 36 He was busy writing letters with a reed upon strips of parchment
. 37 The writings could be rolled into a little hollow, wooden cylinder and unrolled at pleasure
. 38 I saw men and youths frequently entering the house, and seeking employment
. 39 Andrew engaged them as couriers to convey to Philip and his half brother Jonathan, also to Peter and the others at Gennabris, letters notifying them that Jesus would go to Capharnaum for the Sabbath and engaging them to meet Him there
. 40 Meanwhile a messenger arrived from Capharnaum begging Andrew to solicit Jesus to go thither right away, for a messenger from Cades had been there awaiting Him for the past few days
. 41 This man wanted to ask Jesus for help
. 42 Accordingly, with Andrew, Saturnin, Obed, and some of John’s disciples, Jesus set out from the fisher house near Tarichaea to Capharnaum
. 43 This last named city was not close to the lake, but on the plateau and southern slope of a mountain
. 44 On the western side of the lake, the mountain formed a valley through which the Jordan flowed into the lake
. 45 Jesus and His companions went separately, Andrew with his half-brother Jonathan, and Philip—both of whom had come in answer to his notification—walked together
. 46 Jonathan and Philip had not yet met Jesus
. 47 Andrew spoke enthusiastically to them
. 48 He told them all that he had seen of Jesus, and protested that He was indeed the Messiah
. 49 If they desired to follow Him, he added, there was no need of their presenting to Him a formal petition to that effect; all they had to do was to regard Him attentively, and He, seeing their earnest wish, would give them a hint, a word to join His followers
. 50 Mary and the holy women were not in Capharnaum itself, but at Mary’s house in the valley out-side the city and nearer to the lake
. 51 It was there that they celebrated the feast
. 52 The sons of Mary Cleophas, Peter, James the Greater, and his brother John had already arrived from Gennabris with others of the future disciples
. 53 Chased (Nathanael), Thomas, Bartholomew, and Matthew, however, were not present
. 54 But there were many other relatives and friends of the Holy Family who had been invited to Cana for the wedding, celebrating the Sabbath here, because they had been notified that Jesus was expected
. 55 Jesus along with Andrew, Saturnin, some of John’s disciples, Lazarus, and Obed, stopped at a house belonging to the bridegroom Nathanael
. 56 Nathanael’s parents were dead
. 57 They had left a large patrimony to their son
. 58 The future disciples, just come from Gennabris, experienced a certain shyness in Jesus’ company
. 59 They were actuated in this by the influence Nathanael Chased’s opinion had over them and then again, by the thought of the wonderful things they had heard of Jesus from Andrew and some others of John’s disciples
. 60 They were restrained also by their own natural bashfulness and likewise by the remembrance of what Andrew had told them; viz., that they were not to make advances themselves, but merely pay attention to the teaching of Jesus, for that would be sufficient to make them decide to follow Him
. 61 For two whole days had the messenger from Cades been waiting here for Jesus
. 62 Now he approached Him, cast himself at His feet, and informed Him that he was the servant of a man of Cades
. 63 His master, he said, entreated Jesus to return with him and cure his little son who was afflicted with leprosy and a dumb devil
. 64 This man was a most faithful servant; he placed his master’s trouble before Jesus in very pathetic words
. 65 Jesus replied that He could not return with him, but still the child should receive assistance, for he was an innocent boy
. 66 Then He directed the servant to tell his master to stretch himself with extended arms over his son, to recite certain prayers, and the leprosy would disappear
. 67 After which, he, the servant himself, should lie upon the boy and breathe into his mouth
. 68 A blue vapor would then escape from the boy and he would be freed from dumbness
. 69 I had a glimpse of the father and servant curing the boy, as Jesus had directed
. 70 There were certain mysterious reasons for the command that the father and the servant should stretch themselves alternately upon the boy
. 71 The servant himself was the true father of the child, of which fact, however, the master was ignorant
. 72 But Jesus knew it
. 73 Both had therefore to be instrumental in freeing the child from the penalty of sin
. 74 Cades was about six hours from Capharnaum, on the boundary toward Tyre and west of Paneas
. 75 It was once the capital of the Canaanites, but was now a free city whither the prosecuted might flee from justice
. 76 It bordered on a region called Kabul, which had been presented by Solomon to the king of Phoenicia
. 77 I saw this region ever dark, gloomy, dismal
. 78 Jesus always shunned it when going to Tyre and Sidon
. 79 I think robbery and murder were freely carried on in it
. 80 When on the Sabbath Jesus taught in the synagogue, an unusually large crowd was assembled to hear Him, and among His audience were all His friends and relatives
. 81 His teaching was entirely novel to these people, and quite transporting in its eloquence
. 82 He spoke of the nearness of the Kingdom of God, of the light that should not be hidden under a’ bushel, of sowing, and of faith like unto a mustard seed
. 83 He taught, not in naked parables, but with explanations
. 84 The parables were short examples and similitudes, which He used to explain His doctrine more clearly
. 85 I have indeed heard Him in His teaching making use of a great many more parables than are related in the Gospel
. 86 Those there recorded are such as He most frequently used with explanations more or less varied to suit the occasion
. 87 After the close of the Sabbath, Jesus went with His disciples into a little vale near the synagogue
. 88 It seemed intended for a promenade or a place of seclusion
. 89 There were trees in front of the entrance, as well as in the vale
. 90 The sons of Mary Cleophas, of Zebedee, and some others of the disciples were with Him
. 91 But Philip, who was backward and humble, hung behind, not certain as to whether he should or should not follow
. 92 Jesus, who was going on before, turned His head and, addressing Philip, said: “Follow Me!” at which words Philip went on joyously with the others
. 93 There were about twelve in the little band
. 94 Jesus taught here under a tree, His subject being “Vocation and Correspondence
. 95 Andrew, who was full of zeal for his Master’s interests, rejoiced at the happy impression made upon the disciples by the teaching of Jesus on the preceding Sabbath
. 96 He saw them convinced that Jesus was the Messiah, and his own heart was so full that he lost no opportunity to recount to them again and again all that he had seen at Jesus’ baptism, also the miracles He had wrought
. 97 I heard Jesus calling Heaven to witness that they should behold still greater things, and He spoke of His mission from His Heavenly Father
. 98 He alluded also to their own vocation, telling them to hold themselves in readiness
. 99 They would, He continued, have to forsake all when He called them
. 100 He would provide for them, they should suffer no want
. 101 They might still continue their customary occupations, because as the Passover was now approaching He would have to discharge other affairs
. 102 But when He should call them, they should follow Him immediately
. 103 The disciples questioned Him unrestrainedly as to how they should manage with regard to their families
. 104 Peter, for instance, said that just at present he could not leave his old stepfather, who was also Philip’s uncle
. 105 But Jesus relieved his anxiety by His answer, that He would not begin before the Paschal feast; that only insofar as the heart was concerned, should they detach themselves from their occupations; that exteriorly they should continue them until He called them
. 106 In the meantime, however, they should take the necessary steps toward freeing themselves from their different avocations
. 107 Jesus then left the vale by the opposite end, and went to His Mother’s house, one of a row that stood between Capharnaum and Bethsaida
. 108 His nearest relatives accompanied Him, for their mothers also were with Mary
. 109 Very early the next morning, Jesus with His relatives and disciples started for Cana
. 110 Mary and the other women went by themselves, taking the more direct and shorter route
. 111 It was only a narrow footpath running for the most part over a mountain
. 112 The women chose it as being the more private
. 113 It was besides wide enough for them, as they usually walked single file
. 114 A guide went on ahead, and a servant followed at some distance
. 115 Their journey was to the southwest of Capharnaum, almost seven hours
. 116 Jesus and His companions took a more circuitous route through Gennabris
. 117 The road was broader and better suited to conversation
. 118 Jesus taught along the way
. 119 He often halted, gave utterance to some truth, and then explained it
. 120 This road was more to the south than that which Mary took
. 121 It was almost six hours by it from Capharnaum to Gennabris, at which place it turned southward, and three hours more took the traveler to Cana
. 122 Gennabris was a beautiful city
. 123 It had a school and a synagogue
. 124 There was also a school of rhetoric, and the trade carried on was extensive
. 125 Nathanael had his office outside the city in a high house that stood by itself, though there were others at some distance around it
. 126 In spite of the invitation received from the disciples to that effect, he did not go into the city to meet Jesus
. 127 Jesus taught in the synagogue and, with some of the disciples, took a luncheon at the house of a rich Pharisee
. 128 The rest of the disciples had already continued their journey to Cana
. 129 Jesus had commissioned Philip to go to Nathanael and bring him to meet Him on the way
. 130 Jesus was very honorably treated at Gennabris, and the inhabitants were eager to keep Him with them longer
. 131 They brought forward as a reason for His doing so that He was one of their own countrymen, and also that He should have compassion on their sick
. 132 But Jesus soon left them and proceeded to Cana
. 133 Meantime Philip had gone to Nathanael’s office, in which he found several clerks, Nathanael being in a room upstairs
. 134 Philip had never before spoken of Jesus to Nathanael, since he, Nathanael, had not accompanied his friends to Gennabris
. 135 They were, however, well acquainted with each other, and Philip, full of joy, was enthusiastic when speaking of Jesus
. 136 “He is,” he said, “the Messiah of whom the Prophets have spoken
. 137 We have found Him, Jesus of Nazareth, the Son of Joseph
. 138 Nathanael was of a bright, lively disposition, energetic and self-reliant, consequently frank and sincere
. 139 In reply to Philip’s remarks, Nathanael said: “Can anything very good come from Nazareth?” He knew the reputation of the Nazareans, that they were of a contradictory spirit and were not distinguished for the wisdom of their schools
. 140 He thought that a man who had been educated there might indeed shine in the eyes of his credulous and simpleminded friends, but that he could never satisfy his own pretentious claims to learning
. 141 But Philip bade him come and see for himself, for Jesus would soon pass that way to Cana
. 142 Nathanael accordingly accompanied Philip down by the short road to that house which stood a little off the highway to Cana
. 143 Jesus, with some of His disciples, was standing where the road branched off into the highway
. 144 Philip, since Jesus’ injunction to follow Him, had been as joyous and unrestrained as before he had been timid
. 145 Addressing Jesus in a loud voice as they approached, he said: “Rabbi! I bring you here one who has asked: ‘What good can come from Nazareth?'” But Jesus, turning to the disciples who were standing around Him, said as Nathanael came forward: “Behold! A true Israelite, in whom there is no guile!” Jesus uttered the words in a kind, affectionate manner
. 146 Nathanael responded: “How dost Thou know me?” meaning to say: How knowest Thou that I am true and without guile, since we have never before spoken to each other? Jesus answered: “Before Philip called thee, I saw thee when thou wast standing under the fig tree
. 147 These words Jesus accompanied by a significant look at Nathanael intended to recall something to him
. 148 This glance of Jesus instantly awoke in Nathanael the remembrance of a certain passerby whose warning look had endued him with wonderful strength at a moment in which he was struggling with temptation
. 149 He had indeed been standing at the time under a fig tree on the pleasure grounds around the warm baths, gazing upon some beautiful women who, on the other side of the meadow, were playing for fruit
. 150 The powerful impression produced by that glance, and the victory which Jesus had then enabled him to gain, were fixed in his memory, though perhaps the form of the Man to whom he owed both the one and the other had faded from his mind
. 151 Or he may indeed have recognized Jesus without being aware that the warning glance had been designedly given
. 152 But now that Jesus reminded him of it and repeated the significant glance, Nathanael became greatly agitated and impressed
. 153 He felt that Jesus in passing had read his thoughts, and had been to him a guardian angel
. 154 Nathanael was so pure of heart that a thought contrary to the holy virtue had power to trouble his soul
. 155 He recognized, therefore, in Jesus his Saviour and Deliverer
. 156 This knowledge of his thoughts was enough for his upright, impetuous, and grateful heart, enough to make him, on the instant, joyfully acknowledge Jesus before all the disciples
. 157 Humbling himself before Him as he uttered those significant words, Nathanael exclaimed: “Rabbi! Thou art the Son of God! Thou art Israel’s King!” Jesus responded: “Thou believest now because I have said that I saw thee under the fig tree
. 158 Verily, thou shalt greater wonders see!” And then turning to all, He said: “Verily! Ye shall see the heavens open and the angels of God ascending and descending over the Son of Man!” The other disciples, however, did not understand the real import of Jesus’ words concerning the fig tree, nor did they know why Nathanael Chased had so quickly declared for Jesus
. 159 It was like a matter of conscience hidden from all excepting John, to whom Nathanael himself entrusted it at the marriage feast of Cana
. 160 Nathanael asked Jesus whether he should at once leave all things and follow Him, for that he had a brother, to whom he could make over his employment
. 161 Jesus answered him as He had the others on the preceding evening, and invited him to Cana for the marriage feast
. 162 Then Jesus and His disciples proceeded on their way to Cana, Nathanael Chased meanwhile returning home to prepare for the wedding, for which he set out on the following morning.


Chapter 3

The Wedding at Cana

1 Cana, situated on the west side of a hill, was a clean, pleasant place, not so large as Capharnaum
. 2 It had a synagogue to which were attached three priests
. 3 Near it was the public house at which the wedding was to be held
. 4 It had a forecourt planted with trees and shrubs
. 5 From this house to the synagogue, the street was adorned with leafy festoons and arches from which hung garlands and fruits
. 6 The festal hall extended from the entrance of the house back to and beyond the fireplace, a high wall with ledges in it, which was now adorned like an altar with vases and flowers and gifts for the bride
. 7 Almost a third of this spacious hall was behind the fireplace, and there the women sat at the wedding banquet
. 8 The beams supporting the upper story were likewise hung with garlands, and there were means of ascent in order to light the lamps fastened to them
. 9 When Jesus with His disciples arrived near Cana, He was most deferentially received by Mary, the bride’s parents, the bridegroom, and others that had come out to meet Him
. 10 Jesus with His familiar disciples, among them the future Apostles, took up His abode in an isolated house belonging to the maternal aunt of the bridegroom
. 11 This aunt also was a daughter of Anne’s sister Sobe
. 12 She held the mother’s place to the bridegroom during the wedding ceremonies
. 13 The bride’s father was named Israel and was a descendant of Ruth of Bethlehem
. 14 He was an opulent merchant, who carried on a large freighting business
. 15 He owned warehouses and great inns and storing places along the highroads for supplying caravans with fodder
. 16 His employees were numerous, for most of the inhabitants of Cana earned their living by working for him; in fact, all business transactions were wholly in the hands of himself and a few others
. 17 The bride’s mother was a little lame; she limped on one side and had to be led
. 18 All the relatives of St
. 19 Anne and Joachim had come from around Galilee to Cana, in all over one hundred guests
. 20 Mary Marcus, John Marcus, Obed, and Veronica had come from Jerusalem
. 21 Jesus Himself brought about twenty-five of His disciples with Him
. 22 Long ago had Jesus, in His twelfth year at the children’s feast held in the house of St
. 23 Anne upon His return from the Temple, addressed to the bridegroom words full of mysterious significance on the subject of bread and wine
. 24 He had told him that at some future day He would be present at his marriage
. 25 Jesus’ participation in this marriage, like every other action of His earthly career, had, besides its high, mysterious signification, its exterior, apparent, and ordinary motives
. 26 More than once had Mary sent messengers to Jesus begging Him to be present at it
. 27 The friends and relatives of the Holy Family, judging from a human view, were making such speeches as these: “Mary, the Mother of Jesus, is a lone widow
. 28 Jesus is roaming the country, caring little for her or His relatives, etc. etc
. 29 It was on this account, therefore, that Mary was anxious that her Son should honor His friends by His presence at the marriage
. 30 Jesus entered into Mary’s views and looked upon the present as a fitting opportunity to disabuse them of their erroneous ideas
. 31 He undertook also to supply one course of the feast, and so Mary went to Cana before the other guests and helped in the various preparations
. 32 Jesus had engaged to supply all the wine for the feast, wherefore it was that Mary so anxiously reminded Him that the wine failed
. 33 Jesus had also invited Lazarus and Martha to Cana
